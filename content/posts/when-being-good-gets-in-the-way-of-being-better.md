A Tuesday afternoon like any other. I caught myself doing it again. I was breaking the news to the marketing team I couldn’t look at their campaign performance data because I was neck-deep in building a new seasonal reporting dashboard. A dashboard that, if I’m being honest, nobody had _actually_ asked for or, as it turns out, wanted or needed.

I was keeping up with my work — but my schedule was fairly packed. Every sprint was a careful balance of promised deliverables and quick fixes, with just enough documentation to keep my future self from cursing my present self too much. From the outside, everything looked fine. Tasks were getting done, stakeholders were getting their reports, and I was even managing to squeeze in some “stretch projects” between the urgent requests.

But I was always under pressure and never felt that my work really mattered.

### The comfortable lie

I’d gotten pretty good at justifying my choices. When urgent requests came in, I’d point to my current project — usually something technical and measurable — and explain how it would “improve our data quality” or “create long-term cost savings.” These weren’t lies, _exactly_. Every project I chose had real value.

The problem was what I wasn’t doing. My choices had a high opportunity cost.

The marketing team needed to understand why their email conversion rates had dropped 15%, but I was busy building a multi-touch attribution model. The logistics team needed insights into warehouse overstocking, but I was deep into developing a fancy forecasting model.

Each excuse was perfectly valid. Each project was genuinely useful. And each choice was slowly pushing me further from the work that could actually make a difference.

### The thing about being “Good”

Here’s what took me embarrassingly long to realize: I was using “good” work to avoid harder work.

It’s called _moral licensing_ — a psychological phenomenon where doing something good makes us feel more permission to make questionable choices later _\[Merritt, Effron, & Monin, 2010\]_. Like going to the gym in the morning and using that to justify ordering takeaway for the third night in a row. (Not that I’ve ever done that, of course…) or working on something someone might find useful to avoid getting outside your comfort zone.

In my case, I was using legitimately good projects as permission slips to avoid the scary ones. The ones where I might have to admit I wasn’t sure about something, or where the answers weren’t clear cut, or where I might actually fail.

> In my case, I was using legitimately good projects as permission slips to avoid the scary ones.

### Something close to an AHA moment

The moment it hit home was during a performance review with my manager. Looking at my completed projects list, I realized I could split them into two categories:  
\- Technically impressive projects that were nice to have  
\- Simple but crucial analyses I’d rushed through when I couldn’t avoid them anymore and the deadline was closing in

The AHA moment? The rushed analyses had more impact than all my “improvement projects” combined. That beautiful forecasting model? Gathering dust. The quick analysis of overstocked warehouses? Led to immediate process changes and real cost savings.

### Small changes compound over time!

I’d love to tell you I had an epiphany and transformed overnight. I didn’t. What actually happened was smaller, slower and messier. I started with three simple questions before picking up new work:

1\. “_What am I avoiding by choosing this task?_”   
2\. “_Who needs this work, and what decision will it drive?_”  
3\. “_Would I be proud to explain this choice in my next 1:1?_”

Sometimes the answers were uncomfortable. Often, I went ahead with the comfortable choice anyway. But just being aware of the pattern started to shift things. The scarier projects often turned out to be less terrifying than I’d built them up to be. Not easier, mind you — just less terrifying.

Like when I finally tackled that marketing team’s conversion rate problem. Instead of hiding behind lovely attribution modeling, I spent a day diving into their basic funnel metrics. Found a data quality issue that was causing qualified leads to not be counted. Not technically impressive but it helped them adjust their campaign strategy and recover their conversion rates.

### The takeaway

I still catch myself doing it sometimes — reaching for a safe technical project when there’s harder, messier, more important work to be done. The difference is now I usually catch myself doing it. It’s become one of my most helpful topics in 1:1s with my manager — discussing how to balance the technical work I enjoy with the high-impact work that makes a difference.

If you’ve read my previous post on using the LNO framework, you might recognize this as a classic case of prioritizing Leverage work over Neutral work. Those “safe” technical projects often fall into the Neutral category — important but not urgent. The scary, impactful projects? They’re usually Leverage work — the stuff that changes how the business makes decisions.

Remeber, it’s not about never choosing the comfortable option. Sometimes you need those projects — the ones where you know exactly what you’re doing and can deliver with confidence. They’re like a mental palate cleanser between the tough challenges and can be equally as important.

The trick is knowing when you’re using them as a shield to avoid some of that pesky and challenging personal growth.

Next time you find yourself reaching for a “good” project instead of a scary one, ask yourself what you’re actually choosing. Sometimes the most important growth happens in the projects that make you just a little bit nervous.
