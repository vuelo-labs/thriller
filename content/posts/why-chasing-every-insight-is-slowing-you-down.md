Know when to go deep and when to keep it simple.

_I’ve worked across industries — from customer support at Intercom to supply chain management at Microsoft, to helping leaders at LearnUpon make data-driven decisions. My approach? Master the fundamentals, ignore the hype, and focus on what works to deliver valuable work._

It’s easy to think that deep dives into data will lead to magical insights, but more often than not, those **rabbit holes** aren’t where your time is best spent. What stakeholders need is **a bit of fuel to make decisions**, not endless exploration. Enter **analysis paralysis** — you get stuck overthinking, trying to pick the perfect path, when what’s really needed is a clear, direct answer.

Let’s be clear: **“good enough” doesn’t mean cutting corners**. It means knowing when to focus, when to move on, and when a piece of work genuinely needs more attention, like if you hit a data quality issue. So, how do you sidestep the traps of overanalysis and keep things moving?

#### Talk to People First

When someone asks for a deep dive or a vaguely defined analysis, that’s your cue to **pause and clarify**. Before getting dragged down a rabbit hole, take five minutes to talk it out. Ask a few simple questions:

-   _“What decision are you trying to make?”_
-   _“What do you need this number for?”_
-   _“Is there something specific you’d like to know more about?”_
-   _“What do you suspect the answer is going to be?”_

Nine times out of ten, they’re after data to back up a specific point — they just haven’t said it out loud. That quick chat doesn’t just help you avoid wasting time — it **builds trust**. Stakeholders will appreciate that you care enough to get things right, and it’s a great way to strengthen relationships without needing to bend over backwards.

#### It Has to Tie to a Business Objective

Even if the request sounds straightforward, ask yourself: _“Does this tie to a business goal?”_ If it doesn’t, don’t be afraid to push back or push it down the list. You’re not a machine churning out data for the sake of it — your time and skills are too valuable for that.

Being upfront about where a task fits in your priorities isn’t just self-management; it’s about **maintaining trust**. People respect clear boundaries. It’s far better to say, _“Not right now, but I’ll get to it later,”_ than to promise everything and end up delivering half-baked results.

#### Deliver What They Want (Without Falling Down a Rabbit Hole)

Once you’ve clarified the request, **stick to it**. If they’ve asked for a specific number, give them exactly that — don’t overcomplicate it by exploring every possibility. Your job is to **reduce the time to decision**, not create more work. Remember, they’re not after a full research report; they want actionable insights to keep things moving.

Of course, during your analysis, you might spot something interesting — a trend, an anomaly. **Don’t get sidetracked** unless it directly impacts the task. If it doesn’t, **make a note of it** and move on. You can always revisit it when there’s a bit more time.

#### Data Quality vs. Rabbit Holes: Know the Difference

There’s a big difference between chasing rabbit holes and spotting **data quality issues**. If something raises questions about data accuracy, that’s not a rabbit hole — it’s a red flag. **Speed and accuracy** are both critical, but if the data can’t be trusted, you’ll undermine all the trust you’ve built.

Say you see duplicate entries or mismatched fields in your joins — these aren’t quirks; they could seriously throw off your results. If looking into them is going to slow things down, **flag it** to your stakeholder and explain the delay. It’s far better to be upfront than to deliver dodgy numbers that could lead to bad decisions.

If they just need a directional number, ask them. Maybe the pre-investigation stats will do for now, and the exact figures can follow later. On the other hand, if it’s something small — like a formatting error you can quickly fix — sort it, **make a note**, and move on. You’re balancing **speed with accuracy**, but always prioritise reliability.

#### Capture Rabbit Holes for Later: Write Them Down

Just because you’ve spotted an interesting insight doesn’t mean you should chase it right now. But it doesn’t mean you should ignore it either. **Write it down** — the faintest ink is better than the strongest memory.

You _will_ forget it. Revisit it during your **personal postmortem** when you’ve got time to think more deeply without the pressure of a deadline. This isn’t just about keeping track of ideas — it’s a great way to show that you’re thinking strategically. You can then bring these ideas to your **1:1** with your manager and see if they’re worth pursuing.

#### Sell the Idea: Pitch It in Your 1:1

Once you’ve jotted down those rabbit hole ideas, don’t let them gather dust. **Use your 1:1s** to bring them up with your manager, but don’t just mention them in passing. **Sell them properly** — tie them to business goals and show why they matter.

Here’s how you pitch it:

1.  **Explain where the idea came from**: Set the scene, explain what triggered the thought.
2.  **Show the value**: Highlight the potential business impact. Could it solve a problem or open up an opportunity?
3.  **Suggest next steps**: Offer a clear recommendation. Should it be explored now, or kept on the back burner? Having a plan shows you’ve thought it through.

Framing your ideas this way shows you’re **thinking strategically** and staying relevant to business goals. That’s how you build trust and credibility, especially when following up on personal postmortems.

#### Keep It Simple, Stay Focused, and Build Trust

The key to avoiding analysis paralysis isn’t about rushing — it’s about **staying focused**. Talk to people first to clarify what they need. Make sure every task ties back to a business objective, and if it doesn’t, don’t be afraid to say no or push it down the list.

Deliver exactly what’s been asked for, without getting lost in unnecessary rabbit holes. If you spot something interesting, **note it for later**. You’re not ignoring it — you’re prioritising. And if it’s worth exploring, pitch it during your **1:1**.

**Data quality issues**? That’s a different story. If something undermines your accuracy, **flag it immediately**. Speed is important, but **accuracy is essential**.

Here’s a quick recap:

-   **Talk to people** to clarify and build trust.
-   **Tie tasks to business objectives** — if not, say no or push to the backlog.
-   **Deliver what they ask for**; don’t get sidetracked.
-   **Flag data quality issues immediately** — accuracy first.
-   **Note interesting ideas** and pitch them later in your 1:1.

The goal is simple: **make decisions easier**, **keep quality high**, and **build trust along the way** — without getting lost down the rabbit hole**.**
