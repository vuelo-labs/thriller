> _“So, what have you actually finished this quarter?”_

My stomach dropped. The question hung in the air during my quarterly review, each second of silence making my collar feel tighter. I had a dozen projects “in progress,” yet as I sat across from my manager, I couldn’t name a single completed piece of work. The spreadsheet in front of me blurred slightly as I shuffled through my mental list of everything I was working on — all hovering around 60–80% done, none of them actually finished.

I was two years into my transition to an analytics career, and despite having a supportive manager, I still felt perpetually behind. Being busy wasn’t the problem — I was plenty busy. But I was starting to realize that busy doesn’t equal productive. As researchers at Stanford University have confirmed, attempting to juggle multiple cognitive tasks can reduce productivity by as much as 40% (Ophir, Nass & Wagner, 2009).

### Making progress is good right?

Every day started with good intentions and a seemingly manageable task list. The morning would begin with quiet focus, but then the quick questions would come in, the “small” requests would pile up, and somehow I’d end each day with more in-progress work than I’d started with. According to attention researchers at the University of California, each of these interruptions costs us 23 minutes of focused work time (Mark, Gudith & Klocke, 2008). I was burning hours without realizing it.

In stand-ups, I’d become a master of the vague update: _“Making good progress on the customer analysis,”_ I’d say, carefully omitting that it was the same update I’d given last week. My colleagues were polite enough not to point this out, but their _“Any idea when that’ll be done?”_ questions had started carrying a weight that made my chest tighten slightly.

### Learning a new way

My initial solution was to work harder. Longer hours, faster responses, more parallel work streams. I created elaborate tracking systems, color-coded priority matrices, and detailed time blocks. I was organizing my chaos rather than eliminating it. Research from Princeton Neuroscience Institute explains why this approach failed: our brains have a finite capacity for simultaneous tasks, and forcing them to juggle multiple objectives actually impairs our ability to focus on any of them (Cohen & Princeton Team, 2014).

_“You’re thinking about this backward,”_ my manager said during a one-on-one, her voice carrying that gentle tone that meant wisdom was coming. _“Instead of asking how to do more things at once, ask yourself why you’re doing so many things at once.”_

She had me list out everything I was actively working on. Twelve projects. Twelve. Each one important, each one “urgent,” each one perpetually almost done. When she asked me to estimate the actual focused time needed to complete each one, the truth hit like a physical weight: most would take only 2–3 hours of dedicated work to finish.

I wasn’t lacking time — I was lacking focus. This aligns with research from Harvard Business School showing that knowledge workers spend only 27% of their time on skilled, focused work (Gardner, Matviak & Lambert, 2018).

### Learning to finish

The change started small. One Monday, I decided to ignore everything except finishing our customer retention report. Just that one thing. The anxiety of not responding to new requests immediately was real — studies show this anxiety is linked to what researchers call “fear of missing out” or FOMO (Przybylski et al., 2013). But by lunchtime, something remarkable had happened: I had completed the analysis. Not almost done, not making progress — actually done. It just took 2hrs of focused work.

The relief was immediate and tangible. In our team catch-up, instead of my usual “making progress” update, I had something real to show. The positive feedback was nice, but the real reward was crossing something off my list completely.

### A simple system

Over the next few weeks, a pattern emerged that aligned with what productivity researchers call “serial unitasking” (Newport, 2016):

-   Each morning, I’d choose one thing to finish — not work on, but finish!
-   New requests got a clear response: “I’ll start that on \[specific day\]”
-   Every time I felt the urge to switch tasks, I’d remind myself how good it felt to actually finish something and I had that one thing to do.

Progress wasn’t linear. Some days I’d catch myself slipping back into old habits, spreading my attention too thin. But increasingly, I could catch myself doing it. The simple question _“What needs to be finished today?”_ became my anchor.

### Managing real work

Not every task can be finished in a day. But even large projects can be broken down into completable chunks. This approach is supported by research on goal-setting theory, which shows that specific, achievable sub-goals lead to better outcomes than general intentions (Locke & Latham, 2002).

My manager helped me see that clear boundaries actually build more trust than vague promises. When someone asked for something new, I’d check my commitments and give them a realistic start date. Most people appreciated the honesty more than an immediate “yes” followed by weeks of “almost done.”

### And then it becomes worth it

Three months after that uncomfortable quarterly review, I had a different story to tell. Not only could I list my completed projects, but I could also give clear timelines for upcoming work. The constant background anxiety of multiple unfinished tasks had been replaced with the steady rhythm of regular completion.

The irony wasn’t lost on me: by trying to do less at once, I was getting more done than ever. This paradox is well-documented in productivity research, which shows that limiting work-in-progress leads to faster completion rates and better outcomes (Little’s Law in knowledge work contexts, Anderson, 2010).

### A continuous practice

Even now, I sometimes feel the pull of multitasking creeping back in. The desire to say “yes” immediately to new requests never completely goes away. But I’ve learned to recognize the warning signs — that familiar feeling of being busy but not productive, of having lots of motion but little progress.

When I catch myself slipping, I think back to that quarterly review and ask myself: “What do I want to be able to say I’ve finished?”

Failing that. Stick a single stickie note to your desk. Every so often look down and make sure what you are doing supports that task.

Sometimes the best productivity insight isn’t a complex system or a clever hack. Sometimes it’s just building strong processes that help you manage your cognitive load.

_I’ve worked across industries — from customer support at Intercom to supply chain management at Microsoft, to helping leaders at LearnUpon make data-driven decisions. My approach? Master the fundamentals, ignore the hype, and focus on what works to deliver valuable work._
