After 10 years of turning data into decisions, I’ve learned one fundamental truth: the most sophisticated analysis in the world means nothing if nobody acts on it.

The time I spectacularly missed the mark in front a leadership team illustrates this perfectly. I’d spent weeks building what I considered to be a significant piece of analysis. The methodology was robust. The visuals were works of art and aligned perfectly with the story I wanted to present.   
I was properly pleased with myself.

Three slides in, a senior leader stopped me with a question that would, fundamentally, change how I approached stakeholder communications from then onwards: “Ok this all looks good. What should we actually do differently?”

In that moment, I realized I’d answered every question except the one that mattered.

### Misunderstandings occur… quite often

The hard truth they don’t teach you in data science courses: your job isn’t to demonstrate analytical brilliance. **Your job is to help people make informed decisions.**

This subtle distinction marks the difference between being technically correct and actually useful. I’ve watched many analysts fall into the same trap — myself included. We treat every presentation like a doctoral defense when what our stakeholders really need is a clear path forward.

### Actually Being Useful

Years of learning this lesson the hard way (across everything from supply chain to customer success) have taught me to distill stakeholder communication down to four essential questions:

1\. Good or Bad: Is this something we need to worry about?  
2\. Big or Small: How important is this compared to our other challenges?  
3\. Now or Later: What’s the urgency here?  
4\. Next Steps: What’s the single most important action we should take?

Everything else goes in the appendix. Yes, even your lovely methodology slides. (I know, it hurts. We’ll get through this together.)

### Complex before complicated

A particularly illuminating mistake from my own journey perfectly captures this challenge. I spent three days crafting what I thought was a compelling story about user behavior patterns. The analysis had everything — cohort analyses, user journeys, behavioral segmentation and a touch of benchmarking. I’d woven together this intricate tapestry of user interactions across multiple touchpoints.

The stakeholder’s actual need? “Are users happy or not, and what’s the first thing we should fix first?”

In my haste to answer all the questions that I could think of, I missed the most important question to answer.. the one that I was being asked.

### Speaking the same language

Teams across an organization speak fundamentally different languages — not just technically, but in terms of what matters to them. Finance teams live and breathe numbers, but they care about very specific ones. Product teams want to know about user behavior. Sales teams want to know about conversion rates.

My own wake-up call came after spending ages analyzing purchasing patterns for the a recent online sale, walking through every nuance of our customer journey and highlighting upsells and brand adjacencies.   
Their actual need? Did we have more high value customers shopping in the sale than last year. Everything else was just noise and a nice to know… aka a time sink.

**For leadership**: “High-value customer transactions up 15% year-over-year, driving an additional €800k in revenue”

**For the merchandise team**: “Premium brands showing 40% higher sell-through when placed next to our core range”

**For marketing**: “Customers who engaged with the early access email spent twice as much”

**For commercial**: “Average basket value increased by €50 when we bundled complementary products”

Same data, different languages.

### Aim for clear and brief communication

The most powerful phrase in an analyst’s arsenal? “The key thing you need to know is…”

Not “Let me walk you through our methodology…” Not “First, some context about our approach…” Just the key thing they need to know.

Keep your technical details in your back pocket. Yes, you should have them. No, you shouldn’t lead with them.

An unexpected truth about building influence: your reputation as an analyst often grows faster when you’re known for clarity rather than complexity. People remember — and appreciate — when you make their cognitive load lighter. **When you become known as the person who can take complex problems and distill them into simple, actionable insights, you’ll find yourself invited to more important conversations.**

The evidence speaks for itself: I’ve watched analysts struggle for months to build credibility through technical excellence, while others earned trust in weeks by simply making decisions easier for their stakeholders. The formula is beautifully simple — people appreciate thinking less and getting to the point faster.

### **There is nothing wrong with silence**

The real value of leadership presentations lives in what happens after you stop talking.

Most analysts feel compelled to fill every silence with more explanation, more numbers, more context. Resist this urge. When you spend less time talking, you create space for the discussions your analysis should spark.

These discussions reveal everything. Sometimes they’ll declare your analysis brilliant. Sometimes they’ll call it complete rubbish. More often than not, they’ll take your insights and use them to inform some seemingly unrelated decision that leaves you wondering what just happened or send you away to look at some aspect in more detail. Your carefully crafted recommendations might get washed away in the tide of broader business concerns — for now, at least.

**The true opportunity lies in these moments. You’re getting a front-row seat to how high-level decisions actually get made. These opportunities don’t come along often early in your career. Take advantage of the chance to listen and learn.**

My first meeting with senior leaders taught me this lesson. Fighting the urge to jump in with more detail about my methodology, I forced myself to stay quiet and listen. Those twenty minutes taught me more about our company’s strategic priorities than six months of analyzing data.

### Grow with simplicity

The beautiful irony of expertise reveals itself over time: when you stop trying to prove your worth and focus on delivering useful insights, people actually value your work more. This pattern emerges consistently across every organization I’ve encountered.

When you reliably give stakeholders what they need to make decisions:

-   Trust your judgement more
-   They come back with bigger, more important questions
-   They start involving you earlier in decisions
-   They associate you with clarity and action, not complexity and confusion.

And yes, you end up doing less work because you’re not overcomplicating everything

Remember that presentation I mentioned at the start? The next day brought a complete revision. No methodology slides, no statistical tests, just a single page with what needed to change, why and who will be affected. The senior leader’s response? “Now that’s what I needed to know.”

Professional growth often means learning what to leave out. A friend once shared wisdom that transformed my approach: “If they trust you enough to ask your opinion, trust them enough to want it.”

The path forward becomes clear: your analysis might be complex, but your recommendations shouldn’t be. Building your reputation happens not through displays of technical brilliance, but through consistently making it easier for others to make good decisions.
