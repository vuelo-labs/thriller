_I’ve worked across industries — from customer support at Intercom to supply chain management at Microsoft, to helping leaders at LearnUpon make data-driven decisions. My approach? Master the fundamentals, ignore the hype, and focus on what works to deliver valuable work._

I’ve seen this problem from both sides. As an analyst, I’ve received countless vague requests for “quick numbers.” Later, as a manager in supply chain, I watched teams get frustrated trying to interpret unclear requirements. Both experiences taught me the same lesson: **unclear requests waste everyone’s time**, regardless of the industry or role.

Let me tell you about a time when a seemingly simple request landed in my inbox: “Can you pull some customer sales numbers for tomorrow’s meeting?” No context, no specifics — just that. Being eager to help, I dove right in. Three days later, I’d built a comprehensive dashboard showing every sales metric we tracked sliced by multiple segments and other dimensions. The response? “Oh, I just needed to know how the average spend in my department for last week.”

That moment changed how I triage and handle requests. It wasn’t just about the wasted time, it was a little but, it was mostly about realizing that most requestors aren’t trying to be vague. They usually have a clear picture in their head. They just need help getting it onto paper (in a way that’s useful to you).

### Understanding what teams really need

Different teams typically care about different things. WARNING: Keep in mind — this is a broad generalisation. Every company and industry has its quirks but these core priorities tend to hold true whether you’re in tech, retail, manufacturing, or healthcare.

**Sales Teams** They live and die by their funnel. Every request ties back to revenue, leads, or pipeline health. If they’re asking for something that doesn’t connect to closing deals, dig deeper.

**Marketing** Everything revolves around proving return on investment. When they ask for general usage data without mentioning acquisition costs or campaign performance, there’s usually more to the story.

**Customer Success** They’re focused on keeping customers happy and spotting problems before they lead to churn. Their questions often boil down to identifying at-risk accounts or understanding what makes successful customers stick around.

**Operations** They’re all about efficiency and process improvement. Whether it’s supply chain, manufacturing, or service delivery, they want to know where the bottlenecks are and how to fix them.

**Finance** The bottom line drives everything. Cost, revenue, margins — that’s their world. A finance request that doesn’t mention money should raise eyebrows.

**Product Teams** They need to know if people actually use what they’re building. Feature adoption, user behavior, engagement patterns — these are their bread and butter.

**Manufacturing** They care about output, quality, and efficiency. When they ask for “production data,” they’re typically looking to improve yield or reduce waste.

### Turning vague requests into clear action

When that vague request lands (and it will), take five minutes to ask questions. I’ve never had anyone mind, in fact people will actively make time if you show you are willing to listen. Most appreciate that you’re trying to get them exactly what they need.

The real trick isn’t just knowing what to ask — it’s helping people articulate what they actually need. Chances are they’re not being vague on purpose; they just don’t know how to ask for what they want and end up being less specific than you need them to be.

### Red flags to watch for

Over time, you start to spot the signs that a request needs more clarity:

-   The scope keeps growing: “While you’re here, could you just…”
-   The impossible deadline: “Need this for a meeting in an hour!”
-   The request that doesn’t fit their pattern
-   The vague “just curious” requests

Hear these… you should probably put some time on their calendar.

### Making it work in practice

Every vague request is an opportunity to understand the business better and build stronger relationships. The most valuable team members aren’t just those with technical skills — they’re the ones who can turn vague requests into clear action plans.

Its worth mentioning at this point that sometimes requests will come in that are fundamentally ambiguous or new to the business. In these cases, try to understand the questions that the requestor is trying to answer and start from there.

### Pulling it together

When the to-do list feels endless and requests keep coming in vague, remember these key points:

**Get to the Real Ask** Start with “What decision needs making?” Get clear on deadlines and impact. Every vague request hides a real need — your job is finding it.

**Know the Warning Signs** Watch for growing scope, impossible deadlines, and the classic “just curious” requests. They’re all signals to pause and ask questions.

**Keep It Simple** Take time to understand, be honest about timelines, and focus on what moves the needle. Sometimes a simple number beats a complex dashboard.

Remember: Good analysts don’t just provide data — they help make better decisions.
