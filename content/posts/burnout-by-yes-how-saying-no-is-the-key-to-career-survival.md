_I’ve worked across industries — from customer support at Intercom to supply chain management at Microsoft, to helping leaders at LearnUpon make data-driven decisions. My approach? Master the fundamentals, ignore the hype, and focus on what works to deliver valuable work._

If you think saying “yes” to every request will fast-track your success, you might need to revamp your thinking. It’s more likely fast-tracking you straight to burnout. The more you say yes, the more you drown in low-value work that’s eating away at your time, your energy, your career and, ultimately, lowering your quality of life.

The truth? Learning when and how to say **“no”** is one of the most powerful skills you can develop. It’s not about being difficult or trying to slow progress — it’s about protecting your time for work that matters.

#### Why saying “Yes” to everything is actually hurting you

Saying yes all the time might make you feel like you’re the dependable one and someone that can be thought of as the “go to person”. But in reality, you’re setting yourself up to be everyone’s safety net — and it’s wrecking your productivity.

1.  **You Become the Blocker**: If you keep piling on tasks, you end up slowing everything down. People will stop coming to you for solutions and start seeing you as **THE** bottleneck.
2.  **Rushed, Messy Work**: Taking on too much means you can’t give anything the attention it needs. So instead of delivering top-quality work, you’re producing rushed, half-baked work.
3.  **You Lose Focus on What’s Important**: When your day is filled with low-priority tasks, the high-impact stuff gets lost in the shuffle. You’re not making the difference you could be because you’re too busy with the noise rather than the signal.
4.  **Burnout on the Horizon**: Keep saying yes, and you’ll burn out fast. And when you’re burnt out, everything suffers — your health, your work, your career and your home life.

#### The LNO Framework: A mental shortcut to help say No

Here’s your shortcut to smarter decisions: the **LNO Framework**. Use it to decide what’s worth your “yes” and where you need to drop a “no.”

**L = Leverage**: Will this task make a big impact? If it moves the needle for you or the business, it’s a “yes.”

**N = Neutral**: These are tasks that need to be done but don’t create much impact. Say yes if you’ve got the bandwidth, but they shouldn’t be your priority.

**O = Overhead**: Overhead tasks are busy work — the ones that waste time without adding much value. These deserve a hard “no.” Your time is too valuable for this.

#### Expectation Setting: How to say “No” without making fr-enemies

Saying no doesn’t mean you’re closing the door. It’s about **setting boundaries** while maintaining relationships. To say NO without making it awkward while maintaining a postive relationship, there are a few approaches that I’ve found to be useful.

**Offer to help (a little)**: If you can’t take something on, offer to get them started or point them in the right direction. You’re still helping, but you’re not overloading yourself.

**Suggest Someone Else**: If you can’t do it, point them to someone who can. It’s a no for you, but you’re still solving their problem.

**Be Honest About Trade-Offs**: Let them know if taking on more means something other piece of work has to wait. People appreciate the honesty, and it keeps your workload in check.

**Be specific on scope**: If you can’t say no outright, try to negotiate the timeline or reduce the scope. It keeps the project moving without putting too much on your plate.

**If all else fails..** your manager can help you to figure out the best course of action. Let them help you by suggesting other approaches or by stepping in to speak with the requestor about where their request sits in the team priorities.

#### Being cagey about who you say “No” to

Not all no’s are equal. There’s a big difference between saying no to a colleague and saying no to someone higher up. Be strategic about **who** you say no to, especially when relationships are key.

The trick? Focus on delivering high-impact work to the right people. Build trust by saying yes to leverage tasks, and you’ll have more room to say no to the low-priority stuff.

#### When saying “No” starts to bring benefits

Saying no is about protecting your time and making sure you’re focused on the most meaningful work. Here’s a few situations when a well-placed NO can save your mental bandwidth and move your career forward.

**Low-Impact Tasks (Overhead)**: If the task doesn’t support business objectives, it’s probably not worth the time. Politely decline and focus on what actually drives results. Every “no” to overhead gives you more time for high-leverage work.  
**_Note_**_: Its good to speak to the person if you’re unsure. The request could be more impactful but they have explained it poorly. Do your due diligence in asking these questions so you don’t say NO to something that should’ve been a yes._

**Conflicting Priorities (Neutral)**: When your plate is full with high-leverage work, neutral tasks should take a backseat. Saying no here lets you focus on what truly matters without getting bogged down by tasks that won’t move the needle.  
**_Note_**_: Sometimes I like to take some Neutral type work. Leverage tasks are typically a bit more involved with complex requirements. Some simpler requests that let you complete some work are good to take sometimes. This will be based on workload, deadlines and how long the request will take._

**Vague Requests**: If someone can’t clearly explain what they need, it’s a flag that a conversation needs to happen. Ask for more details, and get clarity. You’re protecting yourself from wasting time on half-baked requests that could lead to frustration for everyone involved.  
**_Note_**_: Always speak with the requestor before saying No. Hearing them out and then giving them the reasons that you are not prioritising their ask is respectful and gives them the option to escalate on their side if needed._

#### The Power of Prioritisation

Here’s the secret: saying no isn’t about shutting down opportunities — it’s about **choosing the ones that matter most**. When you focus on high-leverage tasks and ditch the overhead, you make room for work that actually drives results.

Saying no isn’t a negative thing. In fact, it’s a superpower. When you start being more selective, you build a reputation for delivering quality work, not just being the person who says yes to everything.   
**People remember the results you bring and that time that you made them wait for weeks, NOT how many tasks you take on.**

#### Final Thoughts

Saying “no” isn’t about being difficult — it’s about **protecting your time and energy** for the work that truly matters. Use the **LNO framework** to guide your decisions and set clear expectations. Remember, you’re not rejecting opportunities — you’re making space for the ones that will have the biggest impact.

The more strategic your no’s, the more impactful your yeses will be. Saying no isn’t closing doors — it’s making sure you’re walking through the right ones.
