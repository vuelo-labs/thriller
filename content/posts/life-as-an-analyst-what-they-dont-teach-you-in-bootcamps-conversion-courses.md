I’ve worked across multiple industries and roles — from AI-powered projects at Intercom to supply chain management at Microsoft, to helping execs at LearnUpon make data-driven decisions.   
My approach is built on real-world experience, delivering impact by mastering the fundamentals, and focusing on what truly matters. Ignore the hype and focus on what works — everywhere, and forever. Let’s keep it real and deliver work that matters._

So you’ve decided to move into data analytics. You’ve taken the plunge into a conversion course or bootcamp. You’ve done the coursework, learned about programming, BI tools, data visualisation, statistical models, machine learning, maybe even some AI and Big Data tooling, and, crucially, that real data work doesn’t take place in Excel. GASP!

The journey from an early-career analyst to a senior role is more than just a progression of technical skills — it’s about increasing your impact, developing key soft skills, and learning how to prioritise effectively. Along the way, you’ll need to embrace challenges, be patient with your progress, and understand that growth often comes from trial and error. This is the side of life as an analyst that you won’t find in your conversion course syllabus.

Three things that were difficult for me to come to terms with:

-   **Prioritisation** _(what should I work on?)_
-   **Evaluation** _(was it good work?)_
-   **Effective 1:1s** _(how can I do better next time?)_

### 1\. Prioritising Work for Maximum Impact

Let’s face it: when you first step into an analyst role, everything feels important. You’ve got a to-do list that stretches a mile long, and you’re trying to figure out how to do it all. Spoiler alert: you can’t. However, you can learn to prioritise.

-   **The LNO (Leverage, Neutral, Overhead) framework** is originally a Product Manager tool, but it works well for data work too.   
    Repurposing things that weren’t specifically designed for your role is a valuable skill. Try the LNO framework for a while, make some mistakes with it, and if it doesn’t click, try something else. This one is working for me right now:
-   **Leverage tasks**: Align with strategic goals — these push the boat forward.
-   **Neutral tasks**: Necessary but not game-changing.
-   **Overhead tasks**: Time sinks — minimise, delegate, or automate.

**Early on**: Expect to spend a lot of time on neutral tasks — learning the ropes, maintaining reports, performing data checks. You’ll also have some overhead tasks — routine, low-impact work that you can’t avoid.

**As you progress**: The balance shifts. Leverage tasks should take up more of your time as you start leading projects and influencing decisions. Neutral tasks should reduce as you delegate more and automate where possible. Overhead tasks? These should be minimal as you move up, focusing instead on strategy and mentorship.

💡 At the end of the day, some neutral and overhead tasks are necessary. Schedule them for when you need less brain power or when you need a context shift to let your mind breathe a little. You still have to get the work done, so don’t overshoot too early by diving straight into leverage tasks.

### 2\. Evaluating Your Work: Don’t Repeat Mistakes

You’re going to mess up. A lot. That’s the nature of learning. What’s not okay is making the same mistakes over and over and expecting different results. This is where **_Personal Postmortems_** come in. After every major task or project, take the time to reflect:

**Ask yourself**:

-   Did this align with strategic goals?
-   Was my approach efficient?
-   What feedback did I get, and how can I apply it next time?
-   Did I solve the requestor’s problem, or did I just solve the problem I encountered while building a solution? 💡 Beware of the XY problem — make sure you’re solving the issue that was raised rather than figuring out the how to solve it e.g. they want total sales, give them total sales and not a complex dashboard that lets you slice and dice 100 ways and was fun to build.

Reflection isn’t just for your manager’s benefit — it’s for you. Identify patterns, figure out what’s working, and, more importantly, what’s not.

### 3\. Making the Most of Your 1:1s

1:1s with your manager aren’t just a formality — they’re golden opportunities. If you’re only using them to update your manager on what you’ve done, you’re missing out. Your 1:1s should be a chance to get real feedback, set stretch goals, and figure out how to grow in your role.

-   **Come prepared**: Don’t just wait for your manager to tell you what to do — bring your own questions and ideas to the table.
-   **Discuss your personal postmortems**: Work through your findings with your manager. They benefit by helping you improve, so let them do their job!
-   **Ask for feedback**: On specific projects.
-   **Discuss challenges**: How you can tackle new ones.
-   **Be open**: Stepping outside your comfort zone is where real growth happens.

Your manager can offer insights you won’t find in any textbook or course, they’ve been around the block and probably managed people with the same trajectory as you. Listen, be open to suggestions, and use your 1:1s to help the company — and yourself — succeed.

### Get Over Yourself: The Reality of the Job

Not every task is going to be exciting. In fact, a lot of the work you’ll do as an analyst is going to be boring, repetitive, and far from glamorous. This is where you prove your worth. 💡 The analysts who get ahead are the ones who consistently deliver value, even when the work isn’t fun. Be prepared to do the boring work that noone else wants to do.

If you’re feeling disillusioned or lost, take a step back and remember why you got into this field in the first place. Focus on building your skills, learning from your mistakes, and taking on challenges that push you to grow. Seniority isn’t something you’re owed — it’s something you earn by doing the work that needs to be done, day in and day out.

### And Remember, You’re Not Alone

**Data people, in general, are great!**  
We love problem solving, technology, and ideas. Don’t get stuck — there will always be people ready to talk through ideas or give guidance. Remember the point about stepping out of your comfort zone — building that muscle sooner rather than later, and creating a strong network, will make the journey all the more enjoyable. Whether you need advice or just someone to bounce ideas off, your community is there.

### It’s a Long Journey, But Worth It

There’s a lot to learn, but remember — it’s a 10-year journey! The path to seniority is more like a walk than a marathon. Sometimes you’ll move fast, sometimes slow. You’ll take breaks, explore different paths, and even backtrack occasionally.   
💡Everyone’s journey is different, so stay focused on your main quest, but don’t forget to take on side quests too.
