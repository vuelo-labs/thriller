Everyone’s shouting, “urgent!” You’ve got a list of tasks that seems to grow by the minute, and they all sound like they need your immediate attention. So how do you figure out which one actually matters? Welcome to prioritisation — the skill that can make or break your career as an analyst.

Learning to prioritise isn’t just about getting tasks done. It’s about understanding what drives business goals, managing stakeholder expectations, and figuring out how to navigate conflicting requests. And here’s the kicker: **you’ll never fully master it**. But that’s okay. The real goal is getting comfortable with the process, making smart decisions, and asking for input when you need it.

Mistakes? They’re part of the deal. But with time, you’ll learn how to juggle tasks effectively and communicate priorities to your stakeholders with confidence.

Let’s dive into the **LNO (Leverage, Neutral, Overhead) framework**, a tool to help you sort through the chaos, align your work with business strategy, and keep everyone happy — well, mostly.

### The LNO Framework: A Guide to Managing Your Workload

The **LNO (Leverage, Neutral, Overhead)** framework helps you categorise tasks by their business impact, making it easier to prioritise. Here’s the breakdown:

#### Leverage Tasks: High-Impact Work

Leverage tasks are the ones that matter most. These tasks directly support strategic goals, push the business forward, and have a noticeable impact.

**Clearly Leverage**:

-   _Example_: You’re asked to analyse customer churn data for the executive team. This analysis will shape retention strategies and guide key business decisions.
-   _Why it’s leverage_: The task is critical to driving a business goal and has significant impact.

**Not So Clear — Requires Input**:

-   _Example_: A department head asks for a sales prediction model but doesn’t explain how it will be used.
-   _How to approach_: Before assuming it’s leverage, ask if this model will drive decision-making for leadership or contribute to broader business goals.

#### Neutral Tasks: Important…. not Groundbreaking

Neutral tasks are necessary for business operations but don’t directly drive major strategic initiatives. These tasks keep the wheels turning but don’t steal the spotlight.

**Clearly Neutral**:

-   _Example_: Generating weekly performance reports. These provide useful insights but aren’t influencing major decisions right now.
-   _Why it’s neutral_: It’s important to the business, but not urgent or strategically critical.

**Not So Clear — Requires Input**:

-   _Example_: You’re asked to compile a quarterly product performance report, but it’s unclear whether this data will influence any decisions.
-   _How to approach_: Ask how this report will be used. If it’s for reference and not guiding key decisions, it’s probably neutral.

#### Overhead Tasks: Necessary, But Low-Value

Overhead tasks are the ones that suck up time without delivering much value. These include administrative duties or repetitive processes. The goal here is to automate, delegate, or minimise them as much as possible. Sadly, sometimes these are just necessary and need to be part of your workload.

**Clearly Overhead**:

-   _Example_: You get frequent requests to pull the same delivery schedule data for multiple teams. It’s repetitive, and no one is using the data to make strategic decisions.
-   _Why it’s overhead_: This task doesn’t contribute to broader business goals and takes up too much time.

**Not So Clear — Requires Input**:

-   _Example_: A senior manager asks for specific data for an upcoming meeting. It’s unclear whether this request is a one-off or will drive long-term decisions.
-   _How to approach_: Confirm whether this request has broader implications. If it doesn’t, automate it or treat it as a lower priority.

### LNO: Bringing the Band Together

Let’s give the LNO framework some personality. You’ve got your three categories (but how do our merry band stand together?):

**Leverage tasks**: These are the headliners. They’re the reason the concert exists in the first place. Without them, nothing happens.

**Neutral tasks**: They’re the rhythm section. Solid, reliable, and keeping the show going — but no one’s going to remember them.

**Overhead tasks**: These are the roadies. You need them, but if you’re spending too much time here, you’re missing the main event.

### Prioritisation Checklist

Use this quick checklist to figure out where your tasks fit in:

**Does it drive business goals?**

-   Ask: Does this task directly support a strategic initiative?

**Is it a “need to have” or a “nice to have”?**

-   Ask: Will this task create immediate value, or is it more of a background activity?

**What happens if it’s delayed?**

-   Ask: Will the business grind to a halt if this isn’t done right away?

**Can it be automated or delegated?**

-   Action: Automate repetitive tasks or pass them on to someone else.

**How will you communicate your decision?**

-   Action: Set expectations and explain your priorities to stakeholders.

### Dealing with Conflicting Requests

Now let’s apply the checklist to scenarios where conflicting requests come in. You’ve got two tasks that both seem important — how do you decide what takes priority, and how do you handle pushback from the lower priority requestor?

#### Leverage vs. Neutral:

**Scenario**: Inventory Optimisation vs. Marketing Analysis  
You’re asked to optimise inventory for a seasonal campaign, while Marketing wants you to pull data on last year’s performance.

**Understand the strategic goals**

-   _Inventory optimisation_: Critical for avoiding stockouts during a high-sales period. This is a leverage task.
-   _Marketing analysis_: Important but not urgent. This is a neutral task.

**Consult stakeholders early**

-   Marketing confirms their analysis isn’t time-sensitive and can wait. Inventory optimisation takes precedence.

**Estimate the impact**

-   Inventory issues could cost millions in lost sales. Delaying the marketing analysis has minimal immediate consequences.

**Plan for automation or delegation**

-   Automate the marketing data pull to save time. You can handle the deep analysis after the campaign.

**How to handle Marketing’s pushback**

-   _“We need this data to shape strategy for next quarter.”_
-   _Response_: “Inventory is crucial to keeping sales on track for the campaign. I’ve automated the initial data pull for you, and I’ll have the full analysis ready by \[_\*\*INSERT DATE HERE\*\*_\].”

#### Neutral vs. Overhead:

**Scenario**: Stock Reconciliation vs. Ad-Hoc Sales Report.  
You’re responsible for daily stock reconciliation, but a regional manager wants an ad-hoc sales trend report for their area.

**Understand the strategic goals**

-   _Stock reconciliation_: Necessary for operations but not game-changing. This is a neutral task.
-   _Sales report_: A one-off request, which makes it overhead.

**Consult stakeholders early**

-   Confirm with operations that the reconciliation report is essential. The sales report can wait.

**Estimate the impact**

-   Delaying the reconciliation report could cause operational issues. The sales report doesn’t affect strategic decisions.

**Plan for automation or delegation**

-   Automate parts of the sales report, or delegate it to a junior analyst.

**How to handle the regional manager’s pushback**

-   _“I need this sales report to finalise next week’s targets.”_
-   _Response_: “The stock reconciliation keeps operations running smoothly. I can provide a quick summary for you now, and the full report will follow by \[specific date\].”

#### Overhead vs. Overhead:

**Scenario**: Supplier Data vs. Customer Service Report  
Suppliers are constantly asking for delivery schedules, and customer service wants a report on response times. Both requests are overhead.

**Understand the strategic goals**

-   Both are overhead tasks that need to be minimised or automated. Neither is strategically critical.

**Consult stakeholders early**

-   Confirm with both parties that these requests are routine. Consider automating them or creating a self-service option.

**Estimate the impact**

-   Delaying either task doesn’t have immediate consequences.

**Plan for automation or delegation**

-   Build a self-service tool for suppliers to access delivery schedules. Automate the customer service report.

**How to handle pushback from either side**

-   _“We’ve always gotten these reports manually.”_
-   _Response_: “I’m building a self-service tool so you can access this information faster. I’ll guide you through it once it’s ready.”

### Moving from Overhead to Leverage

When you start out, you’ll spend a lot of time on overhead tasks — reports, ad-hoc requests, data pulls. That’s normal. The key is to **master neutral tasks first**. Automate and streamline these tasks to create bandwidth for more leverage work.

As you build trust, stakeholders will start to give you more leverage tasks — the ones that truly push the business forward. The more leverage tasks you take on and succeed at, the more you’ll be seen as a trusted resource who can handle strategic work.

Over time, as you prove yourself and learn to automate or delegate the low-impact work, you’ll transition to more leverage tasks — the ones that make the biggest difference to the business. But don’t forget: **neutral tasks will never disappear completely**. They’re your bread and butter throughout your career, especially early on.

As you progress in your career, prioritisation becomes a more nuanced and essential skill. Moving from overhead tasks to leverage work isn’t just about time management — it’s about focusing on the work that delivers the most impact. Here’s what that shift might look like over time.:

![](https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/800/1*n-HzHzBgimKxUeh8XCgXqg.png)

This table is **guidance** on what to expect, not a strict rulebook.This table is just a guide — everyone’s path looks a little different, but the goal is to **increase leverage work (L)** and **reduce overhead work (O)** as you progress. Here’s what that shift typically looks like:

-   **Early Career (Years 1–2)**: Expect to spend most of your time on neutral and overhead tasks. This stage is about learning the ropes and building trust with your team. Focus on mastering neutral tasks (like reporting and data checks), and find ways to reduce the burden of overhead tasks through automation.
-   **Mid-Career (Years 3–5)**: By this stage, you’ll start taking on more leverage tasks, such as leading analysis projects that shape business decisions. You’ll still be managing neutral tasks, but your goal should be to automate or delegate overhead work to free up time for higher-impact initiatives.
-   **Experienced Career (Years 5–7)**: Now, leverage tasks take up most of your time. You’ll be leading cross-functional projects that directly influence business strategy, while mentoring junior team members to take on the neutral and overhead tasks you once handled.
-   **Senior Career (Years 8–10)**: At this point, you’re focusing almost entirely on leverage work. You’re a strategic leader, driving initiatives that shape the company’s future. Overhead tasks are minimal, as you’ve either automated or delegated them to others on your team.
-   **Advanced Senior (Year 10+)**: You’ve reached a stage where most of your work is high-leverage, high-impact. You’re influencing company-wide strategy, while enabling others to handle the day-to-day operational tasks that used to dominate your workload.

### Work on Shifting Towards Leverage

The main takeaway here is that as you progress, your **focus should shift toward leverage work** — the tasks that push the business forward. Early on, overhead and neutral tasks will be your bread and butter, and that’s okay. Your job is to manage those tasks efficiently, learn from them, and eventually create the space to focus on high-impact, strategic work.

Over time, **prioritisation becomes the tool** that allows you to delegate or automate the low-value work, freeing up your capacity to take on the projects that truly make a difference to the business.

### Why Prioritisation is the Most Crucial Skill for Analysts

Prioritisation is more than just juggling tasks — it’s the key to building trust with stakeholders, aligning your work with the company’s strategic goals, and managing your workload efficiently. As your career progresses, the stakes get higher, and the ability to prioritise becomes critical.

Here’s why prioritisation is a must-have skill:

-   **Builds Trust with Stakeholders**: When you can clearly explain why you’re focusing on one task over another, stakeholders trust your judgement.
-   **Drives Strategic Alignment**: Prioritisation ensures that your work aligns with business goals.
-   **Prevents Burnout**: By automating or delegating low-value tasks, you free up time for high-impact work.

Learning how to prioritise effectively isn’t just about managing tasks in isolation — it’s about building trust, aligning with business goals, and continuously improving. One of the best places to refine your prioritisation skills is by discussing it openly with your manager.

### Using Prioritisation in Your 1:1s

One of the best ways to **refine your prioritisation skills** is by using your 1:1 meetings with your manager to discuss how you’re managing your workload. These conversations aren’t just for status updates — they’re powerful opportunities to get feedback, set stretch goals, and ensure your efforts align with both **personal development** and **business impact**.

Here’s how to make your 1:1s more impactful using the LNO framework:

-   **Discuss Leverage Tasks**: Share the leverage tasks you’re focusing on and explain how they drive key business goals. Ask for feedback on whether these align with broader company strategies.
-   **Review Neutral Tasks**: Talk through any neutral tasks you’ve automated or streamlined. Show how this has created more bandwidth for high-impact work.
-   **Highlight Overhead Tasks**: Identify any overhead tasks you’re handling and explore ways to either automate, delegate, or deprioritise them. Your manager can often help remove obstacles or provide resources to minimise low-value work.
-   **Ask for Input on Prioritisation**: If you’re unsure about certain tasks, ask your manager how they would prioritise them. This not only helps you make smarter decisions, but it also shows you’re proactive in seeking alignment and feedback.

**Pro Tip:** Bring your prioritisation checklist to the meeting! Use it to walk through how you’ve categorised tasks and what you plan to tackle next. This shows clear thinking and ownership over your workload — key traits that managers value.

### Make Prioritisation Your Superpower

Ready to tackle those 10 urgent requests in your inbox? Take 10 minutes, apply the LNO framework, and see what really matters. The rest can wait.

Prioritisation isn’t about juggling everything — it’s about focusing on the tasks that move the needle. The better you get at it, the more trust you’ll build, and the more you’ll be handed work that truly impacts the business.

What’s next? Start applying the LNO framework this week. Pick one project and decide where it falls: leverage, neutral, or overhead. Watch how your ability to make those decisions grows over time, and keep refining your process.
