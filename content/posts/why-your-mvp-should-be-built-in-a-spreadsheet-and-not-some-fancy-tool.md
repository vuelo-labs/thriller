_I’ve worked across industries — from customer support at Intercom to supply chain management at Microsoft, to helping leaders at LearnUpon make data-driven decisions. My approach? Master the fundamentals, ignore the hype, and focus on what works to deliver valuable work._

Just after lunch. You’re ready to ease into your afternoon.   
Then… \*ping\*… someone has a “quick” request — they need some numbers for a meeting and they need it **ASAP**.   
Your first thought? Maybe I should build a dashboard and make it look fancy with tons of filters so they can explore the data themselves.   
The reality? They don’t care about the dashboard that’ll take you two days to build. They just want that number. Fast.

When it comes to being an effective analyst, it’s not about the flashy tools. I get it, tech and tools are fun to learn, play with and help you feel like you’re getting better at your job. For stakeholders, asking you for things, it’s about **speed, accuracy, and giving them what they need**.   
The dirty little secret that everyone knows but won’t acknowledge as true ?   
**Data in a dashboard, nearly, always ends up in a spreadsheet**.   
So, let’s keep it simple and focus on what really matters: delivering results and building trust.

#### Whats an MVP?

MVP stands for Minimum Viable Product — the simplest version of a solution that delivers core value with minimal effort. Strip away the bells and whistles and solve the immediate problem first.

Don’t forget, many times, people will not have fully formed questions, even the most decisive executives are trying to figure stuff out.

#### Why a Spreadsheet?

I’m not saying you should abandon dashboards completely. There’s a time and place for them. But don’t **start** there. When it’s all about speed and accuracy, spreadsheets are your best bet.  
Once you’ve prototyped the analysis and have received feedback and more maybe more questions, you can think about productionising with fancy interactive dashboards.  
When you’re starting, your goal is to deliver what they need, quickly and accurately. Here’s the top 5 reasons that I start with a spreadsheet and why you probably should too:

1.  **Speed**: Spreadsheets let you work fast. No navigating to different platforms, signing in with OKTA or waiting for a refresh to complete. Just plug in the data, run the numbers, and get the answer. That’s what they really want — speed over flash.
2.  **Flexibility**: Excel (or Google Sheets) lets you pivot, filter, and adjust the data however they want. In reality — they’ll probably want to run their own sums. Give them some flexibility to do that.  
    A spreadsheet gives them the flexibility — BI tools don’t.
3.  **Familiarity**: Everyone has used or seen Excel. You’re not asking them to learn something new. Give them something they can open, edit, and play with right away. It’s a no-brainer.
4.  **Trust**: When you’re starting out, you build trust by delivering what people ask for. You get them what they need, fast and accurately.   
    Once they trust you, which can take more time than you’ll appreciate, **then** you get the flexibility to do the fancy stuff.
5.  **Everything Ends Up in Excel Anyway**: In every data role I have had, no matter how sophisticated the tool, someone will always ask, “Can you just put this in Excel for me?” So why not start there?

#### When to Stick with a Spreadsheet

Sure, there’s a time and place for dashboards. But most of the time? Overkill. Here’s when you should keep it simple:

-   **Quick asks**: If they need a single number or a small dataset, don’t waste time with dashboards. Knock it out in Excel and move on.
-   **Static data**: If the data isn’t going to change much, a spreadsheet is perfect. No need for dynamic reports if they just want a one-off answer.
-   **They want control**: Let’s be real — they’ll want to mess around with the data themselves. A spreadsheet lets them do that easily. Dashboards? Not so much.
-   **Building trust**: Early in your career, you don’t build trust by going overboard. You build it by getting them exactly what they need, quickly. Once you’ve got their trust, you can start doing more complex, high-level stuff. But for now? Keep it simple.

#### How to Build an MVP in a Spreadsheet Without Overcomplicating It

Here’s how to throw together a quick MVP in Excel or Google Sheets:

1.  **Understand the ask**: What’s the key number or insight they need?   
    Keep it focused — get them what they need.   
    _For bonus points: when sharing the analysis, ask them if they have any additional questions where you can go a bit deeper._
2.  **Pull the data**: Export your dataset into Excel. This is where you can play with SQL but don’t assume everyone wants to learn some.   
    Keep it clean, simple and easy to digest / understand.  
    _Bonus: add colouring for key fields, use shapes to annotate the data etc.. help to enable them to get value as quickly as possible._
3.  **Run the formulas**: Use SUM, VLOOKUP, or whatever basic functions you need to get the number they want.   
    Simple, fast and accurate, that’s the goal.  
    _Bonus: Add another tab called “Exploratory” where you can dive a little deeper or present some enriched analysis, but don’t distract from the main topic, “time to decision” is what you are trying to minimise._
4.  **Pivot if necessary**: If they need a bit more flexibility, throw the data into a pivot table. It’s quick, it’s easy, and they can slice the data however they like.  
    _Bonus: Offer to show them how to build pivots. Its intuitive, sets you up as an expert and also enables them to start asking more interesting questions… I wonder who they’ll reach out to… maybe some that helped them before…_
5.  **Keep it simple**: Maybe toss in a basic chart if it helps make things clearer, but don’t go overboard. This isn’t the time to show off.  
    _Bonus: Visualisation is a great skill to understand. Try adding a complementary chart, don’t complicate, with some narrative in a text box or practice using pre-attentive attributes to help them read the charts.

#### Final Thoughts:

Give your stakeholders what they want.   
If you gain a reputation for helping them get to answers quickly and simply, you’ll become the go-to person for those kinds of analysis.   
The questions begin getting more complex & strategic and you’ll start to have conversations with more and more senior leadership.  
Who’d have guessed that you can build a successful data career using just SQL & spreadsheets?

Your job isn’t to impress with tools, it’s to get them the answer, and for that, Excel is more than enough.
