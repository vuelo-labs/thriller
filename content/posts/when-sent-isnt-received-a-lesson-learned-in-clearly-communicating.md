> The words “into warehouse” made my stomach turn as realisation hit.

I’d _meant_ late November for customer delivery, but the team had _interpreted_ it as warehouse arrival — meaning customers wouldn’t receive their Christmas orders until January. Not good!

Staring at my screen over morning coffee, the implications of the message from Seattle sank in: “_Good news! I managed to fit in that late order. Scheduled for delivery into warehouse late November. You owe me one!_ 😊”   
Their enthusiasm only made it worse. They’d worked hard to accommodate my request, probably called in favors, only I’d asked for the wrong thing entirely.

They’d been working on this for nearly 12 hours before I’d even seen their confirmation, proud they’d managed to accommodate what they thought I’d asked for.

The implications unfolded in my mind like falling dominoes. Teams across time zones had already spent a full day executing based on this misunderstanding. Suppliers were being contracted for later production dates. Shipping slots were being booked too late. Each step perfectly logical based on their interpretation, each step making the situation worse. My original message stared back at me from my sent folder — how had I not seen this coming?

Running the numbers only confirmed my growing dread. We’d need to expedite everything: rush production, renegotiate shipping slots, compress warehouse processing time. But the real cost wasn’t just financial — we’d need to explain to customers why their Christmas orders would arrive in January. And the worst part? Someone had gone above and beyond to help, thinking they were doing exactly what I needed.

### More than just a simple misunderstanding

We consistently overestimate how clearly we communicate by about 80% (Keysar, 2002). It’s what researchers call the “_curse of knowledge_” — once we know something, it becomes almost impossible to imagine not knowing it. The more I reviewed my original message, the more I saw how my understanding of “_November delivery_” had been completely colored by my focus on customer deadlines. Of course the production team interpreted it through their lens of warehouse operations — that was their daily reality.

In global teams, time zones amplify these misunderstandings. Each hour that passes isn’t just time lost — it’s time spent building on wrong assumptions. Studies show this “_cascade effect_” can double the impact of initial miscommunications (Thompson & Cheng, 2021). By trying to be brief, I’d created a problem that would take days or weeks to unwind.

### Fighting fire with fire

My first instinct was wrong — more details, more meetings, more instructions. As my manager pointed out, “_Clear communication isn’t about saying more, it’s about ensuring what you say lands with the meaning you intend._”

Research shows that when people understand context and implications, they’re 65% more likely to interpret messages as intended (Clark & Brennan, 2018). It’s not about length — it’s about providing the right framework for interpretation.

### Building better bridges

The change wasn’t dramatic, but it was fundamental. Before sending any critical communication, I started asking myself:

-   What does success look like for the end user?
-   What context am I assuming they have?
-   How could this be interpreted differently?

The results were immediate. Instead of “_..rush order with a late November delivery?_” messages became “_For customer delivery before Christmas, we need this to arrive at customers by November 30th, which means…_”

The responses changed too. People started asking clarifying questions not from confusion, but from engagement with the full picture. What initially felt like over-communication actually led to fewer messages overall.

### Before you send important comms

Run through this mental checklist:

-   Who’s the end recipient and what’s their context?
-   What specific action do they need to take and by when?
-   Have I defined terms that could have multiple meanings?
-   Are all dates, times and locations explicitly clear?
-   What assumptions am I making that I haven’t stated?
-   Who else might act on this information?
-   How will they measure success?

### If time is tight…

Focus on three questions (they also fit on a post-it):

-   What exactly needs to happen?
-   By when (in their time zone)?
-   Why is this important?

Here’s how that transforms a message:

Before: “_Can we get that rush order with a late November delivery? Customer needs it for Christmas and I promised we’d make it happen. Let me know if you can work some magic with the production schedule!_”

After: “_For the rush order: we need products to arrive at customer locations by November 25th to meet their Christmas sales commitment. Working backwards, this means we need:_

-   _Products at our warehouse by November 10th_
-   _Production completed by November 1st_

_Can you confirm if this production timeline is possible?_”

Research shows that even this minimal level of context can reduce misunderstandings by over 40% (Martin, 2020).

The irony is clear: taking more time to communicate clearly at the start saves countless hours of clarification later. In a world of global teams and digital communication, the message sent is far less important than the message received.

> Clarity isn’t a nice-to-have, it’s a necessity.

### The real cost of clarity

That Christmas delivery crisis taught me something profound about communication: clarity isn’t a nice-to-have, it’s a necessity. The cost of that one ambiguous message wasn’t just measured in expedited shipping fees or rushed production costs — it was measured in stressed teams, strained relationships, and dented trust.

Yet the solution wasn’t more complex or longer messages. It was about understanding that every word we send can affect someone else’s thoughts and actions. When I write “_delivery_” thinking about customers, and someone reads it thinking about warehouses, we’re not really speaking the same language — even though we’re using the same words.

Now, whenever I catch myself thinking “_they’ll know what I mean,_” I remember that enthusiastic message. Someone trying to help, working hard to deliver exactly what they thought I needed, all based on an assumption about one word’s meaning.

> That’s the real lesson: in professional communication, being understood matters more than being efficient.

Remember the 60 second checklist:

1.  What exactly needs to happen?
2.  By when (in their time zone)?
3.  Why is it important?
4.  What could be misunderstood?

Clear communication takes more time up front, but unclear communication takes far more time to fix. Choose wisely.
