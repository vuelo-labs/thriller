If you think 1:1s are just 30-minute status updates where you recap your to-do list and hope your manager doesn’t ask too many hard questions, I’m here to tell you — you’re missing the point. When done right, 1:1s can become one of the most valuable tools in your professional toolbox. These meetings aren’t just about ticking boxes; they’re about building relationships, growing in your role, and setting the stage for long-term success.

Whether you’re brand new to 1:1s, don’t have them, or just aren’t sure how to get the most out of them, this post will attempt to help you turn that time into something useful. We’ll cover some common mistakes (I’ve made plenty), how to avoid them, and actionable steps to add some intention to your 1:1s.

### What Are 1:1s (and Why Should You Care)?

Let’s start at the start.   
A 1:1 is, typically, a recurring meeting between you and your manager / peer / teammate. In theory, these meetings are designed to help you get feedback, talk about challenges, and plan for the future. In reality, though, they often end up being awkward status reports or informal chats — unless you take control. _(Note: Informal chats are great and necessary. This article just happens to focus on trying to make the most of the professional components.)_

So why should you care about 1:1s? Because these meetings are your golden opportunity to make sure you’re on the right track, tackle issues before they blow up, and discuss your career growth. Done well, 1:1s build trust, clarify priorities, and open up new opportunities. Done poorly (or not at all), they’re just another meeting on the calendar that no one really looks forward to.

### For Those New to 1:1s: A Quick Starter Kit

If 1:1s aren’t already part of your routine, or you’re thinking, “What’s a 1:1 anyway?” — don’t worry, it’s nothing mind blowing.

#### 1\. What if I don’t have regular 1:1s?

If your manager or team don’t schedule 1:1s, don’t just sit around waiting for someone to offer. Take initiative. Ask them if you can set up a recurring meeting, maybe every two weeks or even monthly to start. Keep it short — 15–20 minutes is enough to get the ball rolling.

Example ask: _“Hey \[insert name here\], I’d love to set up a regular 1:1 to make sure we’re aligned and to discuss any feedback or opportunities for growth. Would every second Wednesday at 2 PM work for you?”_

#### 2\. What if I’m not used to structured 1:1s?

Most people I’ve spoken to aren’t, and that’s fine. Start small. Come to the meeting with one or two topics you want to discuss, like feedback on a recent project or a challenge you’re facing. You don’t need a perfect plan — just enough structure to make the meeting productive.

#### 3\. Keep it simple

For your first few 1:1s, try bringing one question about your work and one question about your growth. Example: _“How do you think I handled the last project?”_ and _“Are there any upcoming opportunities where I can develop new skills?”_ or if its with a peer _“How did you overcome a problem that I just had?”_ and _“I like how you did this, can you share your approach or lessons learned?”._

That’s it. No need to overthink it. Just show up ready to engage and learn.

### 1:1s: The Mistakes People (Including Me) Make

Now that you know what 1:1s are (and why they matter), let’s talk about the mistakes people make — and yes, I’ve made them too.

#### Coming unprepared

Throughout my career, I thought the point of a 1:1 was to nod along, give some vague updates, and hope I didn’t get more work. I’d scribble down a few notes five minutes before the meeting, and I never talked about what wasn’t working or wider challenges I was facing. Big mistake. I missed out on feedback that could’ve helped me improve.

#### Filling the time with updates

I used to think the more I talked about what I was working on, the better. I’d ramble on about every little thing, thinking my manager would be impressed. Spoiler alert: they weren’t. Instead, I wasted the meeting, leaving no time for actual feedback or different perspectives.

#### Avoiding tough conversations

If something wasn’t going well, I avoided bringing it up. I figured if I didn’t mention it, maybe no one would notice. Turns out, avoiding problems doesn’t make them disappear. It just makes them worse. Having someone flag them to you is a worse experience than you being brave and bringing them up. Tough conversations are… tough but you can get better at them with practice.

### Where These Mistakes Can Stunt Your Growth

It might seem like these 1:1 missteps aren’t a big deal. But if you keep making them, they’ll seriously hold you back. Here’s why:

#### Missed feedback

If you’re not asking for feedback, you’re letting your blockers win. Your manager likely knows where you can improve, but if you don’t ask, you won’t get that insight. And without it, you’ll keep making the same mistakes — which, newsflash, isn’t great for growth or for your own mental health.

#### Fewer opportunities

Managers aren’t psychic. If you don’t bring up your goals or struggles, they won’t know how to help you — which means you might miss out on new projects or growth opportunities. Don’t leave it up to chance, this also gives you a chance to show you can be trusted with projects.

#### Damaged relationships

Let’s be real — avoiding hard conversations erodes trust. If you only talk about what’s going well, your manager can’t help you tackle the real issues. Relationships are built on honesty, not on polished status updates. These issues tend to blindside teams when not discussed. Nothing will undo good vibes with your manager faster than unexpected bad news.

#### Losing control of your career

If you’re not steering the conversation, you’re not steering your career. By staying quiet or keeping things surface-level, you’re essentially telling your manager to make the decisions for you. That’s how you end up in a career that happens to you, rather than one you actively shape. Do your future self a favour and put a little work in now.

### How My Approach Changed

Once I realised I was wasting my 1:1s, I knew I had to change my approach. Here’s what I did differently:

After each major project, I started doing personal postmortems — basically, reflecting on what went well, what didn’t, and what I could learn. Then I brought those insights to my 1:1s. Instead of just listing my progress, I showed up with questions, ideas, and a clearer sense of what I wanted to improve.

Suddenly, my 1:1s weren’t just status updates. They were valuable conversations that helped me grow and get better feedback from my manager.

### Focus on the Relationship

1:1s aren’t **just** about tasks; they’re also about building a relationship with your manager. And relationships, in case you missed it, aren’t built on 30-minute updates.

#### Be open and honest

If something’s not going well, say so. When I started being upfront about what wasn’t working, my 1:1s became a lot more productive. Asking for help doesn’t make you look weak — it shows you’re invested in doing better.

#### Relax and leave room for small talk

1:1s don’t have to be all business. Take a moment to chat, connect, and engage in a bit of small talk. Trust is built over time, and small moments of connection go a long way toward strengthening your relationship with your manager.

#### Ask how you can help them

Here’s a wild thought: your manager is human too. They’ve got their own challenges. Don’t be afraid to ask, _“How can I support you better?”_ Helping them succeed can create a stronger, mutually beneficial relationship. Plus, it shows you’re a team player with the ability to zoom out from the everyday execution minutia.

### Content for 1:1s: What to Focus On

Now that you’ve built the foundation, let’s talk about what you should actually discuss in your 1:1s. Don’t try to cover everything — focus on 1–3 key topics for each meeting and dive into those. _Don’t overwhelm the person on the other side of the 1:1.  
If you overwhelm your manager with too much, they might start dreading these meetings — and that’s the last thing you want._

Here are a few ideas to start your prep:

#### Feedback from teams

Bring in feedback you’ve received from peers or stakeholders and ask your manager’s opinion. Are you on the right track? What could you improve?

#### Postmortems

After each project, reflect on what went well and what didn’t. Bring these reflections into your 1:1 for a deeper discussion about how to improve for next time.

#### Unblocking challenges

Are you facing a roadblock? Don’t let it fester. Bring it up early and ask for advice on how to overcome it. Your manager is there to help — so use that time to unblock yourself.

#### Career growth

Want to develop new skills? Eyeing a promotion? Talk about your goals and ask for advice on what you should focus on next.

### Set Expectations for How You Want to Run Your 1:1s

If your 1:1s feel all over the place, now’s the time to establish some structure. This is especially important if your manager isn’t used to running structured 1:1s or is not used to you setting an agenda.

#### Prime your manager

Before the meeting, send a note about how you’d like to structure the conversation. Something like: _“I’d like to focus on feedback, blockers, and career growth in our 1:1s. Does that work for you?”_ This helps set expectations and gives your manager a chance to prepare.

#### Consistency matters

Try to establish a regular cadence for your 1:1s. Even if they’re short, consistency helps keep communication open and avoids problems falling through the cracks. Aim for a weekly or bi-weekly schedule, but be flexible when needed.

### Structuring Your Talking Points

Once you’ve got your 1:1s set up, here’s how to structure your talking points:

**Talking Point 1: Feedback on a recent project**

Reflect on your work. What went well? What could have gone better? Ask for feedback and be open to hearing areas where you might need improvement.

**Talking Point 2: Discussing career growth**

Think about where you want to go in your career. Are there opportunities to stretch yourself? Ask your manager for advice on what to prioritise next.

**Talking Point 3: Unblocking a challenge**

If something’s holding you back, whether it’s a technical issue or a difficult colleague, bring it up and ask for advice on how to move forward.

### Follow Through: Action Drives Growth

Here’s the deal — 1:1s only work if you follow through. Your manager is investing time in you, so show them it’s worth it by acting on what you discuss. Repeatedly bringing up the same issues without making progress is a sure way to lose credibility.

-   **Show progress**: Update your manager on how you’ve acted on their feedback.
-   **Make different mistakes**: Sure, mistakes happen. But don’t keep making the same ones. At least make new, more interesting mistakes over time or different versions of the originals.
-   **Build on each meeting**: Use each 1:1 to build on the last, creating a loop of continuous improvement.

### When Your Manager Isn’t Equipped for 1:1s

Not every manager is a natural mentor or incredibly interested in your growth. If your manager isn’t used to structured 1:1s or doesn’t find them valuable, don’t take it personally. Instead, work with them to figure out the best way for both of you to get value out of these meetings.

-   **Work with them**: Offer a simple framework to follow. Start small and build from there.
-   **Secure consistency**: Even if your manager isn’t a natural at mentoring, getting regular time on their calendar is still important. Aim for consistency, even if the meetings are shorter.
-   **Ensure Alignment**: Make sure that the least you are getting from the meeting is some idea of how you are performing vs expectations and how you can improve. You don’t need them to give you a step by step guide (\*_ahem_) but it is always useful to understand where you and work are in relation to business goals.

### Wrapping Up: Make Your 1:1s Work for You and Your Manager

1:1s are an incredibly powerful tool for growth — if you know how to use them. By coming prepared, focusing on key areas, and following through on what you discuss, you’ll build trust and ensure these meetings drive real value for both you and your manager.

And remember, it’s not just about you. Your manager is a resource, but they’re also human. Use your 1:1s to support them, and you’ll find that the relationship — and your career — will flourish..
