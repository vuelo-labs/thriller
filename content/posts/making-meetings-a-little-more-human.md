> _“Listen, we’re just a bunch of people trying to figure shit out.”_

My manager dropped this while reviewing my deck and everything clicked. Twelve slides of methodology, validation and implementation plans for our new segment classification model suddenly felt like… overkill.

The next day, I walked into the leadership meeting and started differently than planned. “_You know when you just can’t think of an easier whay to say something? A simple version will be better._” I said, closing my laptop. “_I think we can drop the twelve slides I prepared and just run through this one-pager instead._”

The room shifted. People who’d been checking emails (_you can tell_) or half-listening suddenly looked up. It wasn’t because I’d said anything particularly brilliant.

> I’d just acknowledged what everyone in that room wanted but rarely got: simplicity.

What followed wasn’t a presentation — it was a conversation. About real customers, real patterns and how a straight forward way of classifying them could help us work better. No methodology or validation deep dives, just practical examples of how this could actually help us.

A nice surprise was this didn’t mean the technical details weren’t important. They absolutely were. In the days that followed, I had deep-dive sessions with various leaders who needed to understand specific aspects of the model. Some wanted to get into the validation methodology. Some needed to understand edge cases. These conversations got properly technical — exactly as they should.

But that initial meeting? That needed to respect everyone’s mental bandwidth. When you have multiple leaders in a room, each carrying their own priorities and challenges, you need to land your message fast. Make it understandable. Give them enough to grasp the value and trust the execution, without drowning them in details they don’t need yet.

That simple line from my manager changed how I thought about business communication entirely. When you strip away the corporate theater, that’s all any meeting really is: people trying to solve problems together. Sometimes that means a one-page overview. Sometimes it means a two-hour technical deep-dive. The trick is knowing which one people need right now.

### The Science behind figuring shit out

This realisation about meetings being spaces where people figure things out together isn’t just anecdotal — it’s backed by fascinating research. Studies in organizational behavior show that when people feel they’re in a collaborative problem-solving environment rather than a presentation setting, engagement increases by up to 67% (Thompson & Morris, 2019). The brain literally processes information differently when we feel we’re working together versus being talked at.

Even more interesting is what happens when you introduce stories — not as narrative devices, but as bridges to understanding. Researchers at Princeton found that when someone shares a relevant experience, listeners’ brain patterns begin to mirror the speaker’s, creating what they call “neural coupling” (Stephens et al., 2010). This isn’t just people paying attention — it’s their brains literally syncing up with yours.

This explains why that simple customer example resonated more than my carefully crafted slides ever could. When we share real experiences, we’re not just transferring information — we’re creating shared understanding. The research shows this kind of storytelling increases information retention compared to just presenting facts and figures (Zak, 2014).

But here’s where it gets really interesting: studies in cognitive load theory show that simplifying complex information doesn’t just make it more accessible — it actually invites participation. When people can quickly grasp the core concept, they’re 64% more likely to contribute their own insights and experiences (Chen & Peterson, 2021). This explains why stripping away those twelve slides of methodology led to more meaningful discussion.

### Back to that meeting

Looking back at that leadership meeting, everything the research suggests played out in real time. The moment I switched from complex slides to simple examples, people started engaging differently. They weren’t just listening — they were adding their own experiences, challenging assumptions, building on ideas.

This wasn’t because I’d suddenly become a better presenter. It was because I’d finally given people what they needed to participate in figuring stuff out together: accessibility and connection. The simple format made the information digestible and the real examples gave them hooks to hang their own experiences on.

### What this means

When we understand that meetings are fundamentally about people trying to figure things out together, everything shifts. Those technical deep-dives I had later with individual leaders? They worked because we’d already established the foundation of shared understanding. The trust was built, the value was clear and now we could get into the details that mattered for their specific needs.

The research and my experience point to the same conclusion: effective business communication isn’t about proving how much you know — it’s about creating spaces where people can work together to solve problems. Sometimes that means sharing a relevant story that builds connection. Sometimes it means simplifying complex ideas so others can contribute.

> **Always it means remembering that we’re just people trying to figure shit out.**

### Moving forward

What I’ve learned since that day with my manager is that every business interaction is an opportunity to either complicate or clarify. When we choose clarity — through authentic stories and simplified information — we create space for real collaboration. The technical details, the methodologies, the validation — they all have their place. But they come after we’ve established that fundamental human connection of people working together to solve problems.

The next time you’re preparing for a meeting, consider this: you’re not there to perform expertise — you’re there to help people figure something out.

-   Share experiences that build bridges to understanding.
-   Keep it simple enough that others can contribute.
-   Trust that the details can come later, once you’ve established that foundation of shared purpose.

Because at the end of the day, we’re all just people trying to figure shit out together. And sometimes, acknowledging that simple truth is the most sophisticated thing we can do.
