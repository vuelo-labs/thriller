Work can be tough. It’s filled with challenges, unexpected twists, and moments of growth _(if you’re willing to learn from them)_. For those of us in analytics, the real fun often comes from pushing yourself to deliver work you haven’t done before. But how do you ensure each project helps you upskill and level up?

It may not be glamorous but it is remarkably effective.

**Personal Postmortems** — a simple yet powerful way to reflect on your work, spot areas for improvement, and help you to align both personal and professional goals with your work.

### Introducing Personal Postmortems

In engineering, a postmortem is a process where a team reviews a project or event after it’s completed. They identify what went well, what didn’t, and how they can avoid pitfalls / repeat successes in the future. It’s a learning tool meant to improve future outcomes by reflecting on past performance.

A **Personal Postmortem** is similar but applied on an individual level. It’s your chance to privately assess your work — what worked, what didn’t, and what surprised you — while also celebrating wins and making notes for future growth. Simple as it sounds, this process has been a game-changer for me ever since I started to apply the framework.

How do you run a personal postmortem?   
Here’s a framework that can guide you through the process, and I’ll show you how I applied it in two very different projects.

### How to Run a Personal Postmortem

1.  **What went well?**  
    Identify what clicked. Whether it was smooth workflows, efficient communication, or solid data, acknowledge your successes. This isn’t just about patting yourself on the back though— it’s about getting to what made these things work so you can replicate them in the future.
2.  **What didn’t go as planned?**  
    Be honest about where things went sideways. Did the project scope change? Did your assumptions prove wrong? Did you take on too much and fall short of your commitments? Acknowledging mistakes without judgement is critical — this is where the learning happens.
3.  **What surprised you?**  
    Surprises often contain valuable lessons or highlight blindspots.   
    Maybe an unexpected challenge threw you off, something went better than expected or someone surprised you by being great or terrible…. Reflecting on these can help you anticipate and respond more effectively in the future.

Here’s how I applied this framework to two real life projects I worked on — one where alignment helped the project succeed and one where a lack of alignment led to missed opportunities.

### Case Study 1: Turning Gut Instincts into Actionable Personas

In an earlier career role, I worked on a segmentation project to help identify customer personas based on gut instincts from the marketing team. It started with a simple, high-level scoping/ discovery meeting, where we outlined suggested personas and their attributes based mostly on intuition and informal qualitative data, after a day of observing customers on site. These personas were meant to shape our marketing strategies, but we had no hard data to back them up.

This project could have easily gone off the rails with endless experimentation, rabbit holes, and deep dives, wasting months on personas no one would use. Instead, I made it a priority to keep communication open with the marketing team, get feedback on the state of play and ensure alignment with their goals throughout the project.

#### What went well:

-   **Regular alignment with stakeholders:** I set up regular check-ins with the marketing team at every major stage. This kept our work aligned with their goals and validated our direction.
-   _How to re-apply:_ Set up structured checkpoints in future projects to ensure consistent alignment with stakeholders. This helps avoid misalignment and keeps the project on track.
-   **Data validation of gut instincts:** We didn’t have much data at the start, but I made sure to validate the personas through rigorous analysis as we gathered more data. This ensured the final personas were both actionable and based on evidence.
-   _How to re-apply:_ When starting with gut feelings or informal insights, always back them up with data. That way, you can ensure your output is grounded and valuable.

#### What didn’t go as planned:

-   **Lack of initial data:** We began the project without solid data, which could have led to wasted time. Fortunately, the regular alignment with the marketing team helped mitigate that risk.
-   _How to avoid this in the future:_ Make sure you have a clear plan for data collection or validation at the start of the project. If data is missing, acknowledge it early and decide how you’ll gather or validate it.

#### What surprised me:

-   **Instincts aligned well with the data:** The marketing team’s gut instincts were surprisingly accurate when we finally validated them with data, leading to quicker iterations and solid results.
-   _How to anticipate this in the future:_ Don’t dismiss gut instincts, but always validate them with data. Sometimes, stakeholders’ intuition can provide a strong foundation, but having data backing it up solidifies the insight.

**Extracting some lessons from this case study:**

-   Ensure regular alignment with stakeholders throughout the project. These check-ins help maintain focus on what matters most to the business.
-   Start with data validation as early as possible. Even if you’re working from gut feelings, ensure you have a process for backing up those instincts with hard evidence.
-   Reflect on surprises. They can teach you how to better anticipate patterns and stakeholder needs in future projects.

By applying the personal postmortem framework to this project, I saw how important alignment and data validation are. It allowed us to turn informal observations into a robust, data-driven model that drove real value for the business.

### Case Study 2: The Missed Opportunity

#### The Situation:

In another role, I worked on a project that leveraged large language models (LLMs) to extract customer sentiment from support tickets. We spent weeks building the framework, validating prompts, and running sample analyses / validation exercises. By the end, we were excited about the insights we’d uncovered around customer pain points and the cost saving impact for the business.

But when we presented our findings to the product team, we found out that many of our recommendations were already on the roadmap. Our efforts, while technically successful, didn’t add as much value as they could have because they weren’t aligned with the team’s priorities and were not familiar with the work they had already done.

#### What went well:

-   **Solid technical implementation:** The framework we built for extracting sentiment data was sound, and the analyses were accurate from a technical perspective.
-   _How to re-apply:_ Keep the framework for future projects. The technical process worked well, so it can be reused, but next time ensure early alignment with stakeholders to focus on solving relevant problems.

#### What didn’t go as planned:

-   **Lack of early alignment with the product team:** We didn’t engage the product team early enough to understand their priorities. As a result, we duplicated work that was already being done, wasting time and effort.
-   _How to avoid this in the future:_ Engage stakeholders early in the project. Make sure to ask about their current pain points and priorities to ensure your work is aligned with existing goals.

#### What surprised me:

-   **Stakeholders valued the process more than the findings:** The product team appreciated the framework we built more than the actual insights. This taught me that sometimes the process can have more long-term value than the immediate results.
-   _How to anticipate this in the future:_ Always consider how the process or framework you build can be repurposed or applied in other areas. Highlight both the results and the long-term utility of the framework when presenting.

**More lessons to learn!**

-   Align with stakeholders early on. This avoids wasting effort on work that may already be underway or duplicating efforts.
-   Recognise that technical success isn’t enough — projects also need to be aligned with business needs to deliver impact.
-   Reflect on surprises. Sometimes the process you build has more long-term value than the immediate findings. Keep this in mind as you frame your work and present it to stakeholders.

By reflecting on this missed opportunity through a postmortem, I learned the importance of early alignment with stakeholders. While the technical solution was solid, the real takeaway was understanding the business context to avoid duplicating work.

### Common Missteps to Avoid

-   **Don’t be too hard on yourself:** This isn’t about self-criticism — it’s about learning. Acknowledge your mistakes, take responsibility, and move on. If you’re too harsh, you’ll struggle to grow / actually run the postmortems. Without reflection, you’ll keep repeating the same mistakes.
-   **Be honest:** Skipping details won’t help you grow. Be honest about where things went wrong, even if it’s uncomfortable. This kind of reflection is what leads to growth. Index on being complete rather than being fast. Each time you run one of these your writing will get better and they’ll take less time.
-   **Don’t skip the postmortem:** It’s easy to move on to the next project without reflecting on the last one, but this is where the learning happens. Write down your reflections, keep a record, and revisit them to track your progress over time. These can be painful but they are worthwhile.

### How Personal Postmortems Support Career Growth

Reflecting on your work doesn’t just help with the next project — it can also help you grow in your role / into your next role. Recently, I was heading into a strategic planning session and felt far more confident than usual because I had taken the time to reflect on a previous project. I knew what went well and had considered how the poorer aspects could be improved and applied in follow-up projects.

That postmortem highlighted key blockers, and I came into the meeting ready to offer insights, point out potential challenges, and demonstrate that I had a deep understanding of the problem area. This led to better alignment with stakeholders, increased trust, and a clearer scope for the next project.

Personal postmortems not only help you improve delivery — they also position you as someone who can execute even in the face of ambiguity or poorly understood project requirements.

### Information Overload? Lets simplify….

1.  **Push Yourself**: Analysts thrive on tackling new challenges. Don’t be afraid to reflect on your work and learn from it — even when things don’t go perfectly.
2.  **Reflect and Write**: After each project, take the time to write down your reflections. Keep track of what went well, what didn’t, and what you learned. This creates a personal growth library you can refer back to.
3.  **Use Postmortems for 1:1s**: Personal postmortems are great material for 1:1 meetings with managers & peers. Share your insights, ask for guidance, and build stronger relationships through constructive discussions. People are generally happy to help you improve, and showing vulnerability is a strength. Controlled vulnerability and a willingness to learn will serve you well throughout your career.
4.  **Review and Recognise Progress**: Looking back on past postmortems can help you spot patterns. If you’re making new mistakes, that’s a sign of growth. If you’re repeating old mistakes, reflect on why and how to adjust.

#### Postmortems don’t have to be complex. The power is in the reflection and the small, consistent improvements.
