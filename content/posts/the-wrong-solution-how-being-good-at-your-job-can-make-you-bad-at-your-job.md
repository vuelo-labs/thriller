“_This is great work… but it’s not what I asked for.”_

I looked up, puzzled. Our Marketing Lead had worked with me on dozens of projects before. His tone was patient, but I felt a flash of frustration — this was exactly what he’d asked for! Wasn’t it?

I turned back to my screen at the massive spreadsheet I’d spent almost two weeks building. 14 tabs of data. I’d pulled it from every system we had (SAP, finance spreadsheets, channel and warehouse inventory, sales forecasts). and added controls to let us cover every scenario I could think of. There were multiple pivot tables comparing stock levels and sales forecasts, broken down by every channel and category we had. I’d even made a tab just to explain what all the other tabs were for and how they work.

> **How could this not be what he wanted?  
> **The problem… it wasn’t what he needed at all.

“_Look_” he said, “_I can see you put a lot of work into this. But I just needed to know where we’re going to miss our targets and by how much. The directors meeting is tomorrow and I still don’t have those numbers._”

That’s when the realisation hit. He’d asked to “_figure out where we’re going to miss_” but I’d heard “_figure out who gets what._” I’d spent two weeks building this allocation guide when all he needed was to see the gaps in our targets. The worst part? A simple table pulling from two systems could have given him those numbers in about 30 minutes.

### How did I get it so wrong?

I couldn’t blame it on working with someone new. If anything, knowing him actually made it worse. Davidson & Roberts’ research found we’re 40% more likely to make assumptions with people we work with regularly. The longer the relationship, the fewer clarifying questions we ask.

The science behind this is really interesting. Thompson’s team at Stanford found our brains actually process information differently with familiar colleagues. With new stakeholders, we listen carefully and ask questions. With familiar ones, we jump straight to pattern matching against past experiences. We literally stop listening carefully to the people we work with most, assuming we already know what they want.

That’s exactly what happened here. When he mentioned missing targets, my brain jumped straight to our past stock discussions. I wasn’t listening to what he was asking— I was pattern-matching it against every conversation about stock levels we’d ever had. I started pulling data before I really understood what he needed, even opening the IDE before the end of the call.

### The human cost.

These mixups hurt more than just our time. Miller & Chen found they create ripple effects through teams and organisations — not through big failures but through small moments where we deliver the wrong thing with great detail. Trust erodes subtly, one misalignment at a time.

He still needed the numbers for his meeting. Now he needed them urgently and we had an awkward conversation to get through first. I suspect you’re like and don’t like making these types of mistakes.

Don’t forget the fact that I had spent almost two weeks on this.

### Step one; fix what was missed.

What he did next changed things and surprised me. Instead of just leaving me to fix it, he simply said “Ok, well let’s figure this out together. Show me what you were thinking and I’ll explain what I need and we cam do this now. Do you have 30 minutes now?”

That conversation changed how I viewed the work that I was being asked to do. We pulled together the right analysis quickly — just one simple table and a clear summary. While we worked, we talked about how we’d missed each other’s meaning so completely. It turned out we both had different ideas about what “figuring it out” meant.

People aren’t trying to trick you with their requests (most of the time).

> They just need “the thing” and they will work with you to get “the thing” that they want.

### Moving passed the mistake.

The fix was simple but not easy. In our next project, I caught myself nodding and opening the IDE to start pulling data. Instead, I caught myself and asked, “_Can you help me understand what decision this needs to support?_”

The conversation completely changed. We spent ten minutes understanding the actual need and saved days of potential rework.   
Research backs this up — technical leads who treat every request as new, even from people they know well, get better results.

> _As Mr Lincoln said “_Give me six hours to chop down a tree and I will spend the first four sharpening the axe_”._

Things improved quickly. I started delivering what was actually needed. Meetings got shorter. Projects needed fewer revisions. There were less clarifying emails. By trying to do less initially, I ended up being more helpful. I tried to give them the least amount possible to make the decision they needed to make.

A few weeks later, in another project meeting, the Marketing Lead grinned as I asked basic questions about what seemed like a simple request. “_You know, these questions would have saved us both two weeks and 13 spreadsheet tabs._” He was right.

### You never really stop learning.

Even now, years later, I sometimes catch myself starting to pull too much data or build too much detail. The urge to show everything we can do doesn’t go away. But just knowing this tendency exists helps avoid it. That Marketing Lead became one of my best work partners. We learned that good collaboration isn’t about showing everything we can do — it’s about delivering what’s actually needed.

### The basic questions?

The key to delivering better technical work isn’t actually technical at all. It’s about slowing down and really listening. Really trying to understand and empathise. Before you open a single spreadsheet or write a line of code, make sure you get answers to these questions:

1.  What decision needs to be made?
2.  When is that decision needed?
3.  What’s the minimum needed to answer this?

These questions have saved me countless hours of building the wrong thing. But the real lesson goes deeper:

> The best solutions come from understanding what others need, not showing what we can do.

**Remember:** Your expertise is valuable, but only if it’s solving the right problem. Take the time to understand what’s really being asked. Your stakeholders will thank you, your work will have more impact and you’ll save yourself from building 14 tabs of the wrong thing.

p.s. The 14 tabs actually ended up being useful for something else. Just because you solved the wrong problem doesn’t mean you didn’t solve someone elses problem.
