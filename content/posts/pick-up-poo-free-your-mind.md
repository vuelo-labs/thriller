### TL;DR (30 seconds)

You’ve got… A LOT on.   
Context switching between tasks, modes, contexts, ideas costs you bandwidth you don’t get back. Fighting traffic — IRL or on the interwebs…  
You can’t escape the switching but you can make it less costly.   
Here are a few things/ ideas that helped me, with a way to test each one today. Take what’s useful, leave whats not.

### Who this is for

Anyone with way too much on. Between work and life and the personal stuff that doesn’t stop arriving, we’ve never had more stuff we have to do or stuff that we get to do. 10 year old me would have killed for the range of stuff my 10 year old gets to do. But we’re still humans. And now we have to interact with machines too, which we’re not really optimised for.

Here are a few tactics I’ve found useful for getting a sense of control back when life accelerates that little bit faster than I’d like. None of these are rules. Just things I stumbled across and tried. Your mileage may vary.

I have a website called [**Linguist**](http://linguist.vuelolabs.com) where I describe a sustainable way to interact with machines cleanly. Useful if it’s specifically that bit of the load you want help with. This piece is wider. The messy human half of any context switch.

### A few things I’ve actually tried

> Things I’ve actually tried, personally. This article is AI assisted but this is based on my lived experience. This is not advice or in any way prescriptive, just how I fared trying this things.

Some require different levels of effort. They get a bit more difficult as you go down the list. Also more impactful. The top is _something for present you_. The bottom is more for _future you_.

If you can, I’d urge you to take this stuff seriously. Maybe not seriously, seriously but probably a little more seriously than you have been up to now.

#### 1\. Three minutes of neck mobility

My firm favourite on the list.   
Is your right shoulder pinned up by your ear? Is your neck tight? Have you been building up to that tension headache at the back of your skull you didn’t quite connect to your last six hours at the desk?

Most of what knocks me out of focus isn’t mental. It’s that I’ve been in the same posture, breathing the same way, with the same six muscles tensed, for hours. The brain catches up to the body.

A short focused movement break resets both. No yoga session. No changing clothes. Three minutes and a timer.

Mark Wildman’s neck routine (link below) is actually life changing. Maybe your neck is fine and your hips are sore, theres desk stretches for those too. Either way, don’t forget about your body when freeing up mental space.

> **_3-min practice:_** _Right now, before you read another section. Set a three-minute timer. Six neck movements, 30 seconds each. Left-right, up-down, ear-up, cell phone drill, Janet Jackson slide, half-circles. Then come back._

_Finding:_ [_Mark Wildman’s neck mobility video_](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=K4dmZ5_n6uU)_. The video is actually 3 mins._

#### 2\. Park the task before you leave it

When you switch tasks, especially under time pressure, especially when the previous one isn’t fully closed, part of your brain stays stuck on it. Sophie Leroy called this _attention residue_. Her follow-up work found that a quick note about where you stopped and what’s next reduced it.

What I actually do. Before I close out a session with Claude or start prepping for a meeting, I leave myself a short note to come back to. A list of the next steps that I would do if I was staying on this task.  
 So when I come back, I don’t have to re-decide. I just do the thing.

> **_2-min practice:_** _Next time you’re about to close Claude or Cursor or whatever, stop. Open a notes app. Write the next step(s). Then switch. Once is enough to know. Better yet, if you can, get the bot to write it._

_Finding:_ [_Leroy & Glomb 2018, Organization Science_](https://doi.org/10.1287/orsc.2017.1184)

#### 3\. Protect the small pauses

Work doesn’t shrink when tools get faster. It intensifies. Recent HBR research has the headline. We fill every two-minute gap between meetings with one more thing. Those gaps used to do real recovery work.

Same goes for the meetings. What new gets said in the last ten minutes of a 30-minute call? Try 20. Try 15 with a tight agenda. Try async. The 30-minute default is calendar Tetris, not deliberate design.

Bigger version: leave the room. Five minutes outside an hour if you can manage it. No phone. Just pick up that poo. Your brain has two attention circuits, one that executes, one that orients, and they suppress each other. A day at the desk only feeds the first. The walk lets the other one run. That’s where pattern-recognition lives.

What I actually do. I’ve got an outside office, so two or three times a day I do a poo run. Three to five minutes. Out the back, brief sit-down, walk back. The most unromantic productivity practice in the world. Also the most effective. The body needs the break. The brain gets the walk. Two birds, one biological imperative.

> **_2-min practice:_** _Next time a meeting ends early, don’t fill it. Stand up. Look out a window. Ten breaths. The hard bit is not opening the next tab._

> **_5-min practice:_** _Once today, leave the room. Get outside. No phone. Five minutes._

> **_Bonus 2-min practice:_** _Look at tomorrow’s calendar. Pick one 30-minute meeting that could be 20 or 15. Change it._

_Findings:_ [_Ranganathan & Ye, HBR Feb 2026_](https://hbr.org/2026/02/ai-doesnt-reduce-work-it-intensifies-it) _·_ [_Berman, Jonides & Kaplan 2008, Psychological Science_](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1467-9280.2008.02225.x)

#### 4\. Cross one line at the end of the day

Not a productivity ritual. A separation one. People who keep work and home cleanly separated report less work-home conflict than people who blur them, especially when work demands are high. What matters less is which ritual. There has to be a line. You have to cross it the same way every day.

Happily I have an office now, so I can close and lock the door. Before that it was closing the curtains over my desk in the bedroom (at one point I put a blanket over it but that actually had the opposite effect). Anything to mark a stop point and take away lingering visual cues.

> **_2-min practice:_** _Close the laptop. Don’t just shut the lid and leave it on the coffee table. Put it away. A drawer. A cupboard. A shelf in another room. If you really want to commit, get a box. A boring black storage box. Laptop, charger, work notebook, all of it. In the box. Lid on. That’s the line._

#### 5\. Audit your politeness to the machine

When I catch myself writing “hope you don’t mind…” to Claude, something in my brain has booted up that’s wired for talking to a person. The machine can’t receive any of it. We’re wired for this, especially when we’re tired. The cost isn’t OpenAI’s electricity bill. Warmth is finite. I’d rather have mine for people. This is what I go on, at length about, on the [**Linguist**](http://linguist.vuelolabs.com) website.

> **_2-min practice:_** _Open the last AI chat you had today. Read what you wrote, not what the machine wrote. Count the bits of warmth. Please, thank you, hope you, sorry to, hedging, softening. Don’t judge yourself. Just count. If its more than 0, you can afford to save yourself a bit of social battery._

_Finding:_ [_Epley, Waytz & Cacioppo 2007, Psychological Review_](https://doi.org/10.1037/0033-295X.114.4.864)

#### 6\. Choose the switches that matter

The machine makes everything feel possible. Pick three projects, dispatch them all, run them in parallel, why not. The cost shows up later. You wake up tomorrow with three half-finished things and no clear sense of what actually matters.

Its easy to have 10 different threads open working on different things and feeling like you are achieving quality when its really just volume that reads well. Burnout is a sneaky one and can catch you offguard.

Greg McKeown’s _Essentialism_ is the antidote. Less but better. Know what the one thing is. Let everything else be a calmer kind of negotiable.

This is the move that pairs best with everything above. Park the task, defend the pauses, audit the warmth. None of it matters if you don’t know what you’re protecting it _for_.

> **_2-min practice:_** _Tonight, before you close the laptop, write down the one thing tomorrow that actually matters. Not three. One. Tomorrow, when you’re about to switch into something that isn’t that thing, ask: is this switch earning its place?_

_Finding:_ [_Greg McKeown, Essentialism (2014)_](https://gregmckeown.com/books/essentialism/)

### Why this should matter to you

The pattern underneath all of this. Get the machine to do the remembering. Get it to take the notes, hold your place, transcribe the meeting, track the steps. That’s what it’s for. That’s what it’s good at.

Keep the attention. Keep the warmth. Keep the priorities.   
Keep the messy human bits that can’t be transcribed.

The goal is to come home with energy left.

The machine doesn’t need it. The people you love do.

Go be human…. and pick up some poo.
