## langford-necklace: THE LANGFORD NECKLACE

### SCENARIO
The Langford Diamond Necklace — worth half a million pounds — was the centrepiece of Saturday night's charity gala at the Grand Hotel. By midnight it was gone, lifted from its locked display case while two hundred guests danced in the next room. Hotel security found the case open, undamaged, and empty. Whoever took it had a key.

### BEAT 1
The display case was found open at 12:05am by a security guard on his rounds. No glass broken, no alarm triggered. The lock was opened with the correct key.

### BEAT 2
Hotel management confirms only three keys to the display case exist. One was in the head of security's safe. One was with the exhibition coordinator, who was on stage giving a speech at the time. The third was kept in the night manager's office — and that office was unlocked all evening.

### BEAT 3
CCTV from the main corridor shows no one entering or leaving the exhibition room between 11:30pm and midnight through the front entrance. But the room has a second door — leading to the kitchen service corridor. That camera was conveniently out of order.

### BEAT 4
A kitchen porter says someone told him to take his break early that night — at 11:35pm, five minutes before the necklace disappeared. He can't remember who told him. The kitchen corridor would have been completely empty.

### BEAT 5
Police searched staff lockers and vehicles this morning. Nothing was found in lockers. But one member of staff refused to open their car boot, claiming they'd lost their keys. They were seen in the hotel car park at 11:50pm.

### PROMPTS
R1: Where were you at midnight?
R2: Have you ever taken something valuable?
R3: What do you know about the kitchen?
R4: What's the most suspicious thing you noticed?
R5: Where would you hide something precious?

## empty-frame: THE EMPTY FRAME

### SCENARIO
A small oil painting by Lucian Freud — insured for £1.2 million — hung in the dining room of The Weatherstone Club, a private members club in Mayfair. During last Thursday's annual dinner, sixty-four members sat three metres from the painting for four hours. When staff cleared the room at midnight, the frame was still on the wall. The canvas had been cut out of it. Nobody heard a thing.

### BEAT 1
Staff confirmed the painting was intact at 7:45pm when they did a final check before guests were seated. The empty frame was discovered at 12:10am. The canvas was removed with a sharp blade — clean cuts, no tearing. This was not improvised.

### BEAT 2
The dining room has two exits: the main double doors, where a maître d' was stationed all evening, and a service door behind a curtain near the far end of the table. The service door leads to a back corridor and then out to a private lane. It was unlocked.

### BEAT 3
A waiter noticed that one guest excused themselves from the table twice during the meal — once just after the starter and once during the cheese course. Both absences lasted roughly five minutes. Most guests only left once, if at all.

### BEAT 4
The club's guest book shows all sixty-four attendees signed in. But a staff member found a rolled piece of fabric in the back corridor at 12:30am — a linen napkin from the dining room, tightly wound, as though it had been used to wrap something flat and delicate.

### BEAT 5
One guest arrived carrying a slim leather portfolio case — the kind used for carrying documents or prints. They said it held architectural plans for a renovation project. That guest left through the main entrance at 11:58pm, two minutes before the rest of the party. The portfolio looked fuller on the way out.

### PROMPTS
R1: How did you spend the evening?
R2: Have you ever wanted something you couldn't afford?
R3: Did you leave the table during dinner?
R4: What did you notice about the service door?
R5: What was in your bag when you left?

## missing-will: THE MISSING WILL

### SCENARIO
Gerald Ashworth died three weeks ago at the age of eighty-one. At the reading of his will last Tuesday, the family solicitor produced a document dated just six days before Gerald's death — replacing the original will entirely. The new version disinherits Gerald's eldest daughter and leaves the Cotswolds house to a different family member. The eldest daughter insists her father would never have changed his wishes. Someone in that room put a pen in a dying man's hand.

### BEAT 1
The replacement will is handwritten and signed by Gerald. A handwriting analyst confirms the signature is genuine, though the letter formation is shaky — consistent with someone elderly and frail. Gerald was bedridden for his final two weeks and rarely had visitors.

### BEAT 2
The care home's visitor log shows only four people visited Gerald in his last week: his eldest daughter on Monday, his younger son on Tuesday and Thursday, a family friend on Wednesday, and the solicitor on Friday to witness the new will. The solicitor says Gerald asked to make changes. He did not ask why.

### BEAT 3
A care home nurse recalls that during one visit mid-week, the visitor asked for privacy and closed the bedroom door — unusual, since Gerald liked the door open. The nurse heard conversation but couldn't make out the words. Gerald seemed agitated afterwards.

### BEAT 4
Gerald's personal notebook was found in his bedside drawer. The last entry, dated four days before his death, reads: "They keep asking about the house. I've told them what I want. I don't want to talk about it anymore." The handwriting is steady — stronger than the signature on the new will.

### BEAT 5
The eldest daughter found a voicemail on her phone from Gerald, left two days before his death. In it, he says: "Nothing's changed, love. Don't let anyone tell you otherwise." He sounds tired but clear. The new will was signed the day after this message.

### PROMPTS
R1: How close were you to Gerald?
R2: What matters most to you about family?
R3: Did you visit Gerald that final week?
R4: Have you ever persuaded someone to change their mind?
R5: What did Gerald say to you last?

## night-safe: THE NIGHT SAFE

### SCENARIO
Ciro's is a busy Italian restaurant on the high street. Every night, the shift manager counts the takings, seals them in a canvas deposit bag, and drops them into the night safe in the back office. Last Friday's bag — containing £4,200 in cash — never made it to the bank. The night safe was found open on Saturday morning, the bag gone. Only three people were in the building after closing.

### BEAT 1
The restaurant closed at 11pm. The last customers left by 11:15pm. Three staff members stayed to close up: the shift manager, a senior waiter, and the kitchen closer. The night safe combination was changed a month ago and only the manager and the owner know it.

### BEAT 2
The back office has no CCTV — the camera broke two weeks ago and hasn't been replaced. The owner says the shift manager was responsible for ordering the replacement. That order was never placed.

### BEAT 3
The kitchen closer says they finished up by 11:40pm and left through the back door. The senior waiter says they were polishing glasses in the dining room until about 11:45pm. The shift manager says they dropped the bag in the safe at 11:35pm, then locked up and left at midnight. Someone's timeline doesn't add up.

### BEAT 4
A taxi driver who picks up staff most Fridays says one person asked to be dropped not at their usual address but at a 24-hour storage facility across town. They were carrying a backpack that they didn't usually have.

### BEAT 5
The owner checked the night safe on Saturday morning. It wasn't forced — it was opened with the correct combination. The dial had been wiped clean. But one partial thumbprint was found on the interior of the safe door, at an angle consistent with someone reaching in, not dropping a bag.

### PROMPTS
R1: What was your role at closing time?
R2: Who do you trust in this room?
R3: What did you do after the last customer left?
R4: Have you ever needed money badly?
R5: Why was the CCTV never replaced?

## the-collector: THE COLLECTOR

### SCENARIO
Maxwell Hargreaves hosted his annual library party last Saturday — twelve guests, rare books, good whisky. The pride of his collection, a first edition of Casino Royale signed by Ian Fleming, sat in a glass-fronted cabinet in the reading room. By Sunday morning, the book was gone. In its place sat a £4 paperback copy from a charity shop, spine uncracked. Someone made a swap and walked out with a book worth £60,000.

### BEAT 1
Hargreaves last checked the cabinet at 6pm before guests arrived. The party ran from 7pm until half past midnight. The cabinet was not locked — Hargreaves says he trusts his friends. The charity shop copy still had a price sticker on the back: "St. Margaret's Hospice, £4.00."

### BEAT 2
The reading room opens directly onto the garden through French doors. Three guests were seen smoking on the terrace at various points during the evening. Anyone stepping outside would have had a clear path to the car park — and back — without passing through the main house.

### BEAT 3
One guest arrived with a tote bag, saying they'd brought a bottle of wine. The wine was produced and opened. But the bag was kept close all evening — hung on the back of their chair, never left at the coat rack with everyone else's things.

### BEAT 4
Hargreaves mentioned the book's value during a toast at 9pm. He told guests Fleming had signed it in 1953 and that he'd turned down an auction house offer last year. Within thirty minutes, two guests asked to look at the book up close. The cabinet was opened. After that, nobody remembers if it was closed again.

### BEAT 5
On Monday, a local charity shop volunteer called Hargreaves. She recognised him from the papers. She said someone had come in last Wednesday and bought a Bond paperback — paying cash, very specifically asking for Casino Royale. She remembers because they were well-dressed and it seemed an odd purchase. She thinks she'd recognise them again.

### PROMPTS
R1: What brought you to the party?
R2: Do you collect anything yourself?
R3: Did you handle the book that night?
R4: Where were you after the toast at nine?
R5: What's in your bag right now?

## trophy-cabinet: THE TROPHY CABINET 🏫

### SCENARIO
The Year 6 leaving trophy — a tall silver cup engraved with every leavers' class since 1985 — has vanished from the school trophy cabinet. It was there at morning assembly on Thursday. By the time Mrs Patel went to polish it for Friday's ceremony, the cabinet was unlocked and the trophy was gone. The whole school is talking about it. The ceremony is tomorrow.

### BEAT 1
The trophy cabinet sits in the main corridor, next to the school office. It's locked with a small key that hangs on a hook inside the office. Any pupil sent to the office with a message or a register could have seen where the key is kept.

### BEAT 2
Mrs Patel checked the sign-out sheet. Three classes visited the corridor that day for various reasons. But the office admin remembers one pupil coming in alone during afternoon break to "return a library book" — except the library is on the other side of the school.

### BEAT 3
The school caretaker found a muddy footprint near the fire exit at the end of the corridor. The fire exit leads to the field. It rained on Thursday morning — anyone who went onto the field would have had muddy shoes.

### BEAT 4
A pupil in Year 5 says they saw someone carrying something wrapped in a PE kit bag across the playground just before the end of lunch. The bag looked heavier than usual. They didn't think much of it at the time.

### BEAT 5
The caretaker checked the lost property cupboard in the hall and found a PE bag stuffed behind the coats. Inside it was a cloth that smelled of metal polish — the same brand Mrs Patel uses on the trophies. No trophy inside, but someone had been handling it recently.

### PROMPTS
R1: What do you like about school?
R2: Have you ever borrowed something without asking?
R3: Where were you at afternoon break?
R4: What did you see near the trophy cabinet?
R5: Why were your shoes muddy on Thursday?

## secret-ingredient: THE SECRET INGREDIENT 🧁

### SCENARIO
The school bake sale was ruined. Twelve trays of cakes, biscuits, and brownies were set out in the hall at lunchtime — and almost every single one tasted disgusting. Someone had swapped the sugar in the home economics room for salt before the Year 6 baking session that morning. Mrs Okafor tested what was left in the jar: pure table salt. Forty pounds' worth of ingredients wasted, and the bake sale money was meant to pay for the end-of-year trip.

### BEAT 1
The home economics room was unlocked from 8am. The baking session started at 9:15am. Mrs Okafor set out all the ingredients the evening before, including a large jar labelled "SUGAR." Someone got to it before the class did.

### BEAT 2
The school's morning breakfast club runs from 8am to 8:45am in the room next door. Six pupils attended breakfast club that morning. The connecting door between the two rooms is usually propped open so staff can move between them.

### BEAT 3
A breakfast club helper noticed that one pupil left the table for a few minutes, saying they needed the toilet. The toilets are past the home economics room. They were gone for what felt like longer than usual.

### BEAT 4
Mrs Okafor remembers that the sugar jar felt full when she checked it at the start of class — she even said "lovely, all ready to go." But salt weighs more than sugar for the same volume. If someone had swapped it, the jar would actually feel slightly heavier. She didn't notice.

### BEAT 5
The caretaker found a half-empty bag of table salt in the recycling bin outside the home economics room. It was a brand the school doesn't use — it's sold at the corner shop on the high street. The receipt was still in the bag. It was bought at 7:52am that morning, paid for in cash.

### PROMPTS
R1: What's your favourite thing to bake?
R2: Have you ever played a prank that went wrong?
R3: What were you doing before school started?
R4: Did you go near the home economics room?
R5: Why would someone want to ruin the bake sale?

## time-capsule: THE TIME CAPSULE 🕰️

### SCENARIO
Ten years ago, the Year 6 class of 2016 buried a time capsule under the oak tree in the school garden. It was supposed to be opened at this year's summer fair, in front of the whole school. But when Mr Griffiths dug it up on Wednesday morning, the seal was already broken. The box had been opened and resealed. Inside, everything looked normal — letters, drawings, a class photo — except one thing was missing: a small envelope that the 2016 class teacher says contained something "very personal and very important." Nobody will say what it was.

### BEAT 1
The time capsule was a sealed metal box buried two feet underground. Mr Griffiths noticed the soil above it looked disturbed — looser and darker than the surrounding ground. Someone had dug it up recently and filled the hole back in.

### BEAT 2
The school garden is behind a fence with a gate that latches but doesn't lock. It's visible from the staffroom window but only during school hours. After 4pm, no one overlooks it. A pupil or adult could reach it easily in the evening.

### BEAT 3
A Year 5 pupil says they saw fresh dirt on the path near the oak tree last Tuesday morning — the day before Mr Griffiths dug it up. They thought the gardener had been planting something. The school gardener says he hasn't touched that bed in months.

### BEAT 4
The 2016 class teacher, Miss Yardley, is visibly upset about the missing envelope. She says a pupil who has since moved away put something very personal inside — a letter they wrote to their future self about a difficult time at home. Miss Yardley promised that child no one would read it before the opening. She feels she's broken a promise.

### BEAT 5
The school's side gate has a camera above it. Footage from Monday evening shows a figure entering the school grounds at 6:15pm carrying a small rucksack and what looks like a garden trowel. They leave forty minutes later. The image is grainy, but the figure is wearing a school hoodie — the old design, the one from 2016.

### PROMPTS
R1: What would you put in a time capsule?
R2: Have you ever read something private that wasn't yours?
R3: Were you near the school garden this week?
R4: Do you know what was in the envelope?
R5: Where were you on Monday evening?
